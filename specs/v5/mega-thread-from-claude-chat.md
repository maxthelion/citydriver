Great questions — these get at the heart of how urban morphology actually works in practice.

How Plot Sizes Are Determined
Plot sizes emerge from the interaction of several forces:

Historical subdivision method. Most cities were laid out by landowners or developers subdividing larger parcels. The grain of that original subdivision persists for centuries. A medieval market town has tiny, deep plots (3–8m frontages) because they were optimized for a shopfront + dwelling. A 19th-century suburb has wider, shallower plots because horse-drawn vehicles and social status demanded it.

The "burgage cycle." In medieval towns, the canonical plot was the burgage — a narrow street frontage (one cart-width, ~5–7m) with a long rear garden running back to a lane. This shape was economically efficient: maximize street frontage, which was taxable and commercially valuable. The depth was typically 3–5× the width.

Land economics at time of development. Denser land = smaller plots. In a city centre, a 100m² plot might have been subdivided off a larger estate. In the urban fringe, a developer selling housing lots in 1910 gave each household 600–1000m².

Block geometry as a constraint. Plot width × number of plots must fit within the block, so block depth constrains maximum plot depth. Plots in a 60m-deep block are structurally different from those in a 120m block.

Typical size ranges by typology:

Medieval town centre: 30–80m², 4–6m frontage
19th-c terrace housing (London, Paris): 80–200m², 5–10m frontage
Early 20th-c suburban house: 300–800m²
American suburban lot (postwar): 600–1,400m²
Industrial/warehouse plot: 2,000–20,000m²
How Often Are Plots Merged and Subdivided?
This is one of the most important and underappreciated dynamics in urban morphology.

Subdivision (one plot → many) happens:

When land value rises and density pressure increases
When heirs inherit a property and split it
When a developer buys a house on a large lot and builds flats at the rear
Frequency: common in growing cities, inner suburbs, or areas rezoned for density
Amalgamation (many plots → one) happens:

When a developer needs a large footprint (office, hotel, supermarket, car park)
When a local authority assembles land for redevelopment
When an industrial use expands
Frequency: less common, but happens in 1–3% of the plot stock per decade in active urban areas
The key insight (from Conzen's morphological analysis): plots are the most persistent element of urban form. Street lines last longer than buildings, but plot boundaries last longest of all — sometimes surviving for 500–1,000 years even as buildings are demolished and rebuilt. You can often detect medieval burgage plots underneath Victorian terraces underneath modern flats.

What If a Big Building Needs to Be Built?
Several mechanisms, roughly in order of how common they are:

Plot amalgamation — a developer buys up adjacent plots over time (sometimes secretly via shell companies to avoid holdout problems) and consolidates them. A city block that was 20 plots becomes 4, then 1. This is the standard mechanism for commercial development.
Superblock development — in planned or post-war redevelopment contexts, entire blocks are cleared and rebuilt as a single ownership unit. The original plot structure is erased entirely. This is how most 1950s–70s housing estates and corporate campuses work.
Air rights and vertical subdivision — in some cities (especially the US), the plot stays the same but the building can be much larger through FAR (Floor Area Ratio) bonuses, air rights transfers from adjacent plots, etc. The plot doesn't merge — the development rights are traded.
Compulsory purchase / eminent domain — governments can force amalgamation for public interest projects. Used for transport infrastructure, regeneration zones, etc.
Holdout / awkward remnant plots — often one owner refuses to sell, resulting in the famous "nail house" phenomenon (a tiny old building surrounded by a large development). These either get designed around or eventually acquired.
Are Streets Removed?
Rarely, and it's a big deal when it happens. Streets are the most persistent element of urban morphology — more persistent than even plot boundaries in many cases. A few mechanisms:

Street vacation / stopping up — a legal process where a street is formally removed from the public realm. Requires legal process, compensation, and is politically difficult. Used for: superblock developments, campus expansions, industrial estates.

Pedestrianisation — the street corridor stays, but is removed from the vehicular network. Extremely common in European city centres. The geometry is preserved; only the use changes.

Infrastructure overbuilding — streets are buried (tunnelled) or built over (decked), particularly around rail yards and highways. The street may disappear from the surface while the right-of-way persists underground.

Superblock erasure — in large-scale clearance schemes (Haussmann's Paris, Robert Moses's New York, Soviet microrayons), entire street networks were erased and replaced with a coarser grid. This is historically the main exception to street persistence.

The general rule: streets are almost never removed in organic city growth. They may be narrowed, redirected, or pedestrianised, but the corridor almost always remains because it serves as a property boundary for all the plots on both sides. Removing a street requires simultaneously resolving the ownership and access rights of every abutting plot.

For your city gen system, the implications are probably:

Plot size should be derived from block depth and the era/density of development, not set globally
Amalgamation should be a triggered process driven by demand for large footprints, not random
Streets should be treated as near-permanent — big buildings work around them via amalgamation, not by removing them
Plot persistence means historical layers matter: a "modern" block might still have medieval plot grain visible in its subdivision pattern

Where Do Big Blocks Come From?
Big blocks are almost always the product of single-ownership development at scale, where one entity controls a large parcel and develops it without needing to subdivide for sale.

The main origins:

Institutional land grants. Churches, monasteries, universities, and hospitals were given large land grants and developed them as unified campuses. Oxford and Cambridge colleges, London's Inns of Court, the Vatican precinct — these are all big-block structures because the institution never needed to sell off pieces. The land stayed in one hand.

Aristocratic estate development. In London especially, the great estates (Bedford, Grosvenor, Portman, Cadogan) owned large chunks of the city and developed them under long leasehold rather than freehold sale. Because they retained ownership, they could develop at the scale of the whole estate — producing the large blocks and uniform terraces of Bloomsbury, Belgravia, and Marylebone. The block is big because the landowner never fragmented the freehold.

Industrial and infrastructure uses. Railway yards, gasworks, dockyards, factories — these required large unbroken footprints and were built on land assembled specifically for that purpose. They produce very large blocks because subdivision would defeat the operational purpose.

Planned colonial or military grids. Many cities founded by colonial powers, the military, or planned settlement schemes (Spanish Laws of the Indies towns, American land survey townships, Soviet new towns) were laid out with large blocks from the start. The planner set the block size, and it was large because no organic subdivision pressure existed yet.

Post-war clearance and redevelopment. This is the violent version — existing fine-grain urban fabric was demolished and replaced with superblocks. The Barbican in London, most postwar social housing estates, many American urban renewal projects. The old blocks were erased; new ones were drawn at a completely different scale.

Why Were They Preserved?
This is the more interesting question. Big blocks should be unstable — high land values should cause subdivision over time. So why do they persist?

Single ownership is the key mechanism. If one entity owns the whole block, there's no market pressure to subdivide unless the owner chooses to. And owners often have strong reasons not to:

Institutions don't sell because their identity is bound up in their land. A college that sold off half its quad for housing would cease to be that college. The land is constitutive of the institution.
Estate owners under the English leasehold system retained reversionary interest — the land came back to them when leases expired, which made fragmentation economically irrational. Better to keep the whole and redevelop on your own terms.
Industrial operators couldn't subdivide without destroying the operational logic of the site.
Legal structures freeze the boundary. Entail, trust structures, charitable incorporation, and crown ownership all make land legally difficult to sell or subdivide. A lot of large institutional blocks are protected not by sentiment but by the legal form of ownership.

The block earns more whole than divided. A large estate in a prime location can extract rental income from the whole that exceeds what piecemeal sale would yield, especially if the owner has a long time horizon. Grosvenor's Mayfair estate is worth vastly more as a unified portfolio than as individual sold-off freeholds.

Political protection. University precincts, religious landholdings, and royal parks have planning protection and political capital that prevents compulsory subdivision. Trying to carve up Christ Church Meadow in Oxford or the grounds of Versailles would face overwhelming institutional resistance.

Why Weren't Their Plots Subdivided?
Here the answer splits depending on whether plots existed and weren't subdivided, or whether plots were never created in the first place.

Plots were never created (institutional/industrial model). A monastery garden, a railway marshalling yard, a factory floor — these never had internal plot structure. The block was always a single ownership unit with no internal subdivision into alienable parcels. There's nothing to subdivide because the plot is the block.

Plots existed but weren't released. In leasehold estate development, individual houses were built on plots with their own leases, but the freehold stayed with the estate. When a terrace house was demolished or fell vacant, the estate owner could choose what to do with it. Because they owned all the freeholds, they could amalgamate or rebuild at will — but they also had no incentive to let outsiders in who might fragment the block.

Subdivision pressure never arrived. Big blocks on the urban periphery — or in cities that stopped growing — simply never faced the demand pressure that would make subdivision economically attractive. A large country house block absorbed into a slowly-growing provincial town might sit undivided for centuries simply because there's no developer willing to pay for subdivision rights.

The ratchet only turns one way under fragmented ownership. Once a block is subdivided into multiple freeholds, coordination to re-amalgamate is extremely difficult (holdout problem). But a unified owner faces no such coordination cost. They can hold indefinitely. So big-block preservation under single ownership is actually the low-energy state — it takes active effort to fragment, not to preserve.

The Underlying Pattern
The fundamental variable is ownership structure at the moment of development:

Fragmented ownership from the start → fine grain, small plots, organic subdivision over time, high persistence of plot boundaries
Unified ownership at development → coarse grain, large blocks, plot structure either absent or subordinate to the owner's will, persistence depends on whether ownership stays unified
Cities are essentially a palimpsest of these two regimes layered on top of each other. The medieval core is fine-grain fragmented freehold. The Georgian estate districts are unified leasehold at the block scale. The postwar estates are unified public ownership at the superblock scale. Each regime produces a legible morphological signature.

For city gen, this suggests that block size should be an output of ownership regime and development era, not an independent variable — and that large blocks should carry a flag indicating they're likely single-ownership, which constrains what can happen to them downstream in the simulation.

The ownership-grain relationship is real everywhere, but the specific mechanisms vary enormously. There are a few deep structural differences that produce genuinely different morphological outcomes.

Where the Pattern Holds
The basic logic — unified ownership produces coarse grain, fragmented ownership produces fine grain — is fairly universal. You see it in:

Japanese temple precincts sitting as large unsubdivided blocks inside otherwise intensely fine-grained urban fabric
Waqf (Islamic endowment) land in Ottoman and Middle Eastern cities, which stayed large and undivided because religious endowment law made it legally inalienable
Colonial institutional compounds (mission stations, colonial government quarters, military cantonments) in African and South Asian cities
Chinese danwei (work unit) compounds under Mao-era planning — factories and their attached housing, schools, and clinics occupying large self-contained superblocks
The institutional exemption from market pressure appears to be genuinely cross-cultural.

Key Structural Differences
1. Communal vs. individual land tenure

In much of sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and parts of Latin America, land was traditionally held communally — by a clan, lineage group, or village — rather than by individuals. This produces a very different subdivision dynamic. Land isn't sold; it's allocated by a chief or elder to members of the group. The "plot" emerges from use and allocation rather than legal transaction.

When cities grow rapidly over this kind of tenure, you get informal settlements where plot boundaries are socially negotiated rather than legally registered. The grain can be surprisingly fine and stable — people respect each other's boundaries — but it's invisible to the formal planning system, which creates enormous problems when the city tries to build infrastructure.

2. The absence of a strong leasehold tradition

The English leasehold estate model was historically unusual. Most European countries used Roman-derived property law with strong freehold norms and weak mechanisms for retaining superior ownership. This meant that when French, Spanish, or Italian aristocrats developed land, they typically sold the freehold outright rather than retaining it on lease. The result: faster fragmentation, less persistence of large unified blocks, and a different pattern of subdivision over time.

Haussmann's Paris is the dramatic demonstration of this — because Napoleonic land law made it relatively straightforward to expropriate and reassemble land for public purposes, the state could force amalgamation and street-widening in a way that English common law made nearly impossible. The big blocks of Haussmann's boulevards are a product of state power substituting for the absent unified private landowner.

3. Islamic cities and the waqf system

Ottoman and broader Islamic urban morphology has a genuinely different structure that's worth understanding on its own terms rather than as a variation on European patterns.

The waqf (charitable endowment) system froze large amounts of urban land in perpetuity — mosques, madrasas, hans (caravanserais), hammams, and their surrounding commercial properties were endowed as inalienable religious trusts. This produced large institutional blocks that were legally immune to subdivision or sale, very similar to the English institutional model but operating through religious rather than secular law.

Outside the waqf properties, Islamic urban fabric tends to be extremely fine-grained and organic, with plots that are irregular in shape (because streets follow desire lines rather than planned grids) and deep interior courts rather than street frontages. The street network is often hierarchical in a distinctive way — broad commercial spines leading into progressively narrower residential lanes leading into dead-end culs-de-sac serving a single family cluster. This isn't just aesthetic; it reflects legal norms around privacy, the right to block a lane serving only your household, and the gradual privatisation of public space as you move inward.

4. Chinese and Japanese urban morphology

Both have strong traditions of fine-grained urban fabric, but for different structural reasons.

In China, the imperial grid (as in Tang-dynasty Chang'an or the ideal capital plan) was actually a coarse grid of very large blocks (called fang), each of which was a walled residential precinct rather than a subdivided block of individual plots. The fine grain was inside the block, not visible from the street. Over time, as the fang walls broke down and commercial streets penetrated the interiors, this coarse-block structure fragmented into fine-grained hutong neighbourhoods — which is why Beijing's hutongs have that characteristic pattern of narrow lanes between courtyard compounds.

Japan's traditional urban fabric (especially in merchant districts) is extremely fine-grained — frontages of 3–5m are common — and this reflects a strong commercial culture where street frontage was the primary asset. But Japan also has an unusual relationship with permanence: traditional wooden construction means buildings were rebuilt regularly, so the plot boundary is persistent even while the building stock turns over very rapidly. Japanese cities were also subject to periodic catastrophic fire, which meant the plot structure had to be re-established after destruction — reinforcing its cultural and legal persistence.

5. The American grid and its specific problems

The US land survey system (the Public Land Survey System west of the Appalachians) produced an exceptionally regular grid of townships and sections, subdivided into quarter-sections and then into individual lots. This was land as a commodity from the start — surveyed before settlement, sold as a financial instrument, developed by whoever bought it.

The result is a peculiar combination: very regular block structure at the macro scale (because the grid was planned), but highly variable plot grain within blocks (because individual lot owners developed independently). American cities lack the estate-development tradition that kept large English blocks unified — land was sold freehold from the beginning, fragmented immediately, and the holdout problem became endemic very early.

This is why American urban renewal in the 1950s–70s was so violent. There was no mechanism for gentle amalgamation — the only way to assemble large blocks was government compulsory purchase. The superblocks of American public housing are scar tissue from that process.

6. Rapid urbanisation in the Global South

Many of the fastest-growing cities in the world — Lagos, Dhaka, Kinshasa, Jakarta — are expanding through informal settlement processes that operate almost entirely outside formal planning. Plot structure here is established by first occupancy and community recognition rather than legal subdivision. The grain is typically fine (small self-built plots), but there's enormous variation in regularity.

What's distinctive is the speed of both formation and transformation. Plots that were established informally twenty years ago are now being amalgamated and rebuilt as land values rise. The morphological cycle that took centuries in European cities is happening in decades. And because the formal legal record doesn't match the physical reality, the usual mechanisms for tracking subdivision and amalgamation simply don't apply.

The Deepest Difference
If you had to identify one variable that produces the most divergent outcomes across cultures, it's probably whether the state or the market is the primary agent of large-scale urban form.

In European (especially Anglo) cities, large blocks come from private unified ownership — estates, institutions, companies. The state mostly regulates.

In cities shaped by strong centralised planning — Haussmann's Paris, Soviet microrayons, Chinese danwei compounds, Korean new towns, Singapore's HDB estates — large blocks come from state action, either through expropriation, public land ownership from the start, or direct construction. The state substitutes for the private unified landowner.

In cities shaped by fragmented private markets with weak states — most informal settlements, most 19th-century American development — the default is fine grain, and large blocks only appear when someone (public or private) has the resources and legal tools to force amalgamation.

For city gen, this suggests the most important parameter might not be block size directly, but who controls land at the moment of development — and the block size, plot structure, and subdivision dynamics should all be derived from that.

This is a surprisingly deep question because the answer isn't "rectangular" even though that's the intuitive assumption.

The Fundamental Driver: How Land Was Originally Bounded
Large plots — before any subdivision — are almost never geometrically ideal. Their shape is determined by whatever bounded them at the moment of their legal or customary establishment.

Natural boundaries. Rivers, ridgelines, forest edges, marsh margins, and coastlines were the primary boundaries for large landholdings across almost all cultures before surveying instruments. A lord's estate ran "from the river to the ridge" — the shape was whatever that corridor happened to be. This produces highly irregular, organically curved boundaries that reflect the landscape rather than any geometric intention.

Agricultural strips and field systems. In the open-field system common across medieval Europe, individual holdings were long narrow strips following the plough direction, grouped into furlongs. When these were enclosed — consolidated into single ownership — the resulting plot shape was a bundle of parallel strips, producing characteristically elongated, slightly irregular polygons aligned with the old plough direction. You can still read the ghost of medieval strips in English field boundaries and in the curved property lines of many English towns built over enclosed farmland.

Road and path frontage. Almost everywhere, early landholding was defined relative to the nearest road or path, because that was the primary means of access and the point of legal reference. Large plots were therefore typically deeper than they were wide, with the "front" defined by the road and the rear defined by some natural feature or the edge of someone else's land. The width-to-depth ratio reflects the importance of frontage — valuable frontage was kept short, depth was less constrained.

Adjacency to other holdings. The shape of your plot is partly defined by the shape of your neighbours' plots. If your neighbour has an irregular eastern boundary, your western boundary follows it — not because of any geometric rule but because that's where their land ended. This produces the characteristic interlocking irregularity of pre-survey landholding, where no single boundary can be understood in isolation.

The Rules That Govern Shape
There are several overlapping rule systems, operating at different scales.

The access imperative. Every plot must have access to a road or navigable water. This is not just a planning rule — it's a fundamental economic constraint, because a landlocked plot is nearly worthless. This single constraint does more to determine large plot shapes than anything else. Plots are typically oriented with their narrowest dimension toward the road (to conserve frontage value) and their longest dimension running back from it. Truly irregular landscapes produce irregular plots, but the road-facing orientation is almost universal.

The watershed principle in agricultural land. Large rural holdings that became urban fringe land were often bounded along watershed lines — the natural drainage divides between catchments. This is because water rights and drainage responsibilities followed watershed logic. A holding that straddled a watershed had complicated obligations about where water drained and who was responsible for flooding downstream. The result is that many large plots have one or more boundaries that follow subtle topographic ridges, even in relatively flat terrain.

Least-effort boundary negotiation. When two landowners established a shared boundary, they typically chose the easiest-to-define feature available — a ditch, a hedge, a row of trees, a track. Abstract straight lines require surveying equipment and legal agreement; natural features are self-defining and self-maintaining. This means pre-survey large plot boundaries overwhelmingly follow physical features even when the terrain is mild. The straight property line is actually a late development, dependent on instruments and legal culture.

The survey grid where it was imposed. Where a colonial or state authority surveyed land before settlement — the US township-and-range system, Roman centuriation, Spanish colonial grids — large plots are rectangular from the start, because they were defined by the survey rather than by negotiation with neighbours or natural features. The shape rule here is simple: plots are rectangular subdivisions of a regular grid, with dimensions determined by the survey unit (the Roman actus, the American chain). Irregularities only appear at the edges of the survey grid, where it meets coastlines, rivers, or pre-existing claims.

Ecclesiastical and manorial grant shapes. Church land grants and manorial boundaries in medieval Europe were often defined by perambulation — a physical walk around the boundary, recorded in a written description referencing named features. This produced shapes that were legally defined by a sequence of landmarks rather than by coordinates, which means the shape could be highly irregular and could only be reconstructed by finding all the named features. Many English parish boundaries still follow this perambulation logic, and the large institutional plots derived from those grants inherit the same irregularity.

What This Means for Plot Shape in Practice
If you look at any pre-industrial city and trace the large plots back to their origins, you find a consistent pattern:

The oldest and largest plots — monastery precincts, palace grounds, aristocratic estates — tend to have one straight or formally defined edge (typically the street frontage or the wall facing the city) and irregular back and side boundaries following older landscape features. The formal face presented to the city; the informal rear absorbed the landscape.

Medium-aged large plots — enclosed fields absorbed into urban expansion — tend to be elongated parallelograms aligned with the old agricultural grain, sometimes with slightly curved long sides following plough curvature, with one end facing a road.

Survey-derived large plots are rectangular by construction, but even here the grid is interrupted by rivers, coasts, and prior claims, producing the characteristic triangular and trapezoidal remnant parcels at the edges of any survey grid.

The key generalisation is that shape regularity is a product of state or institutional power at the moment of creation. The more powerful and organised the originating authority, the more regular the shape. Roman centuriation is the most regular land division ever produced at scale. Medieval strip enclosure is moderately regular within furlongs but irregular between them. Organic rural settlement is highly irregular throughout.

For city gen, this has a direct implication: large plots should probably not be generated as rectangles unless you're modelling a survey-grid context. The more historically organic the city, the more large plots should be treated as irregular polygons with one anchored edge (the road frontage) and free-form remaining boundaries derived from terrain features, adjacency with neighbours, and the historical agricultural or natural grain of the land. The rectangular large plot is actually a special case rather than the default.

This is the core design question for the whole system. Let me try to lay out the option space clearly before giving you my actual opinion.

The Two Poles
Type-based approach: You define a set of city archetypes (medieval organic, Islamic medina, colonial grid, Haussmann boulevard, Soviet microrayon, etc.) each with a bundle of pre-tuned parameters. Generation picks or blends types. The types encode historical knowledge implicitly.

Variable-based approach: You define a set of independent axes (climate, topology, tenure system, political structure, era, commercial orientation, etc.) and derive all morphological behaviour from their combination. No explicit types — the types emerge from variable combinations.

In practice most serious generative systems end up somewhere between these, and the interesting question is where and why.

What the Variables Actually Are
Before deciding on architecture, it's worth being precise about what you'd be randomising. The variables that actually drive urban morphology fall into a few distinct categories:

Geographic determinants — these constrain everything downstream and don't change (much):

Topology (flat plain, river valley, hillside, peninsula, island)
Water access (river, coast, none)
Climate (affects building form, street orientation for sun/wind, courtyard vs. outward-facing typologies)
Resource geography (what's nearby that generates economic activity)
Institutional/political structure — this is probably the most underrated variable:

Who owns land at founding (state, church, private individuals, communal)
Tenure system (freehold sale, leasehold, communal allocation, state assignment)
Degree of central planning authority
Fortification requirement (walled city vs. open settlement)
Economic base — drives which uses dominate and where:

Agricultural market town, port, manufacturing, administrative capital, religious centre
This determines which axes carry the most traffic and where the highest-value land is
Cultural norms — affect street/plot/building relationships:

Privacy orientation (Islamic inward-facing vs. European outward-facing)
Street as social space vs. transit corridor
Relationship between residential and commercial (mixed vs. separated)
Era and technology:

Pre-vehicular vs. horse-cart vs. railway vs. automobile — each produces different street widths and block depths
Building technology (load-bearing masonry, timber frame, steel, concrete) affects maximum plot coverage and building height
Growth trajectory:

Slow organic accretion vs. rapid planned expansion vs. repeated destruction and rebuilding
Whether growth was driven by population pressure, economic opportunity, or administrative decision
The Key Structural Problem
The reason this is hard is that these variables don't combine independently. They interact in ways that are highly non-linear:

Topology + tenure + era → block size and shape
Economic base + street technology → street hierarchy
Cultural norms + climate → plot depth and building orientation
Political structure + growth trajectory → planned vs. organic ratio
If you treat them as independent sliders, you'll generate combinations that are internally incoherent — an Islamic privacy norm combined with a Haussmann boulevard street pattern, for instance, or a fine-grain medieval plot structure on a flat American survey grid.

This is the main argument for having some type structure: types encode the coherent historical combinations that actually occurred. The variables within a type can be randomised; the cross-type incoherence is prevented by the type system.

My Actual Opinion on Architecture
I'd suggest a three-layer system:

Layer 1: Founding conditions (mostly fixed per city) A small set of parameters set at city generation time that constrain everything downstream:

Geographic context (topology class, water access type)
Founding era (this bundles a lot — technology, political norms, typical tenure)
Founding institution (who established it and why — port company, religious order, colonial authority, organic market growth, military garrison)
Cultural region (a coarse classification that encodes privacy norms, street relationships, etc.)
These four variables in combination produce a fairly constrained space. A "medieval, river crossing, organic market, northern European" city has a very different morphological logic from a "colonial, coastal, port company, Hispanic" city.

Layer 2: Growth episodes (repeated, variable) Cities don't have one morphological character — they accumulate layers. Each growth episode has:

Era (which determines technology and planning norms in vogue)
Agent (who's doing the building — state, private estate, speculative developer, self-builders)
Pressure (how much growth, how fast — affects how much planning can keep up with demand)
Relationship to existing fabric (does it continue the existing grain, impose a new one, or clear and replace?)
This is where you get the palimpsest quality of real cities. A city might have an organic medieval core, a Georgian estate district added in the 18th century, a railway-driven industrial expansion in the 19th, and a postwar superblock housing estate — each episode with its own morphological rules, layered on the same terrain.

Layer 3: Plot-level rules (derived) From the founding conditions and current growth episode, you derive the actual operational rules:

Typical block dimensions and shape generation method
Plot width/depth distributions and their variance
Amalgamation and subdivision triggers and probabilities
Street hierarchy and width rules
Frontage relationship rules (setbacks, building lines, etc.)
These layer-3 rules are what actually run the generator. But they're derived from layers 1 and 2 rather than set directly, which means you get coherent combinations by construction.

On the Organic vs. Planned Spectrum
Rather than treating this as a binary or even a single axis, I'd model it as two separate variables that combine:

Planning authority — how much centralised control exists at the moment of development (none → weak → strong → total)

Planning intention — when planning does occur, what geometry does it prefer (organic improvement, regular grid, radial, monumental axis, superblock)

Low planning authority + any intention = organic outcome regardless of intention (plans get ignored, modified, overridden by market)

High planning authority + grid intention = American/colonial grid

High planning authority + radial intention = Baroque capital (Paris, Washington, Vienna Ringstrasse)

High planning authority + superblock intention = Soviet/modernist

The interesting cases are medium planning authority, where you get the partially-planned outcomes — Haussmann imposed boulevards but left the existing plot structure largely intact behind them; English estate developers planned the street layout but let individual builders vary the houses.

On Randomisation
Given all this, I'd randomise differently at each layer:

Layer 1 — randomise founding conditions with geographic and cultural coherence constraints. Don't allow incoherent combinations. This is where you use something like a type system but implicitly, through conditional distributions rather than explicit archetypes.

Layer 2 — randomise growth episodes probabilistically based on the city's trajectory. A successful port city in 1850 has a high probability of a railway episode and a speculative development episode; a stagnant agricultural town doesn't. Episodes should feel historically plausible even if not historically specific.

Layer 3 — randomise within the distributions derived from layers 1 and 2. Plot widths vary around the era-and-tenure-determined mean; some blocks are larger or smaller than typical; some streets are wider or narrower. This is where you get the within-city variation that prevents everything looking templated.

The key payoff of this architecture is that the interesting variation — the cultural and historical character of a city — comes from layers 1 and 2, which you can author carefully. The visual variation — the irregularity that makes it feel real rather than generated — comes from layer 3, which you can randomise freely without breaking coherence.

But that's too coarse. The interesting question is what CityState actually contains, because that determines what episodes can read and write, which determines what compositions are possible.

What CityState Needs to Contain
Working from the morphological logic we've discussed, a city state probably needs at minimum:

CityState {
  geography:     Geography          -- fixed, never mutated
  fabric:        UrbanFabric        -- the actual morphological content
  ownership:     OwnershipMap       -- who owns what
  infrastructure: InfrastructureNet -- streets, utilities, waterways
  economy:       EconomyState       -- land values, use pressures, activity centres
  era:           Era                -- current technological/cultural moment
  history:       Episode[]          -- log of what happened
}
Geography is read-only. Everything else is mutable by episodes.

UrbanFabric is probably the most complex — it's the layered spatial record of blocks, plots, buildings, and their morphological character. This is essentially your DCEL or equivalent spatial structure.

Episodes as Functions with Typed Effects
Rather than episodes being opaque CityState -> CityState transformations, you want them to declare what they touch. This makes composition safe and makes the system inspectable:

Episode {
  reads:    StateSlice[]    -- what it needs to read
  writes:   StateSlice[]    -- what it may mutate
  params:   EpisodeParams   -- the specific configuration
  run:      CityState -> CityState
}
So a railway episode might declare:

RailwayEpisode {
  reads:  [geography, infrastructure, economy, ownership]
  writes: [infrastructure, ownership, fabric, economy]
  params: RailwayParams {
    era:            Era           -- determines station typology, track gauge etc
    operator:       Operator      -- state, private company
    entryPoint:     GeoPoint      -- where the line enters the city
    stationCount:   Int
    landAssembly:   AssemblyMode  -- compulsory purchase vs negotiated
  }
}
The reads/writes declaration gives you a dependency graph over episodes, which becomes useful when you want to validate that a sequence is internally coherent, or when you want to parallelise independent episodes.

The Railway Episode Concretely
What does a railway episode actually do? Breaking it into sub-operations:

RailwayEpisode.run(state, params):

  1. RouteSelection
     reads:  geography, infrastructure, economy, ownership
     output: Corridor  -- a right-of-way path through the city
     logic:  least-cost path avoiding high-value developed land,
             preferring flat terrain, river crossings at narrows,
             aiming for the existing commercial centre

  2. LandAssembly
     reads:  ownership, corridor
     output: AcquisitionResult  -- which plots were acquired, at what cost,
                                    any holdouts remaining
     logic:  compulsory purchase clears everything in corridor;
             negotiated purchase leaves holdout plots as obstacles
             that the route must route around

  3. FabricClearance
     reads:  fabric, acquisitionResult
     output: fabric'  -- plots within corridor removed or truncated,
                          remnant plots created at edges

  4. InfrastructurePlacement
     reads:  corridor, fabric'
     output: infrastructure'  -- track, station(s), goods yard placed
                                  stations placed at existing street intersections
                                  where possible

  5. EconomyUpdate
     reads:  infrastructure', economy
     output: economy'  -- land value gradient shifts around stations,
                          new activity centre(s) created,
                          some existing centres weakened

  6. InducedDevelopment  (optional, can be a separate episode)
     reads:  economy', ownership, era
     output: fabric''  -- speculative plots laid out near stations,
                          warehouse plots in goods yard vicinity
Each sub-operation is itself a function with a clear signature. The episode is their composition.

Sub-Operations as Reusable Primitives
The interesting property is that many sub-operations recur across different episodes. LandAssembly appears in railway episodes, estate development episodes, road-widening episodes, and clearance episodes. EconomyUpdate appears in almost everything. InducedDevelopment is essentially the speculative development episode run at smaller scale.

This suggests a lower layer of primitive operations that episodes are composed from:

Primitives:
  routePath(from, to, constraints, costFn) -> Corridor
  assembleLand(corridor, ownershipMap, mode) -> AcquisitionResult  
  clearFabric(plots, fabric) -> fabric'
  placeFacility(location, type, era) -> FabricElement
  updateLandValues(newFacility, decayFn, economy) -> economy'
  subdivideBlock(block, grain, era) -> Plot[]
  amalgamatePlots(plots[], reason) -> Plot
  layoutStreets(area, pattern, era) -> InfrastructureNet
  assignUse(plot, useFn, economy) -> Plot'
Episodes are then compositions of primitives with specific parameter choices. A RailwayEpisode routes a path, assembles land compulsorily, clears fabric, places track and station, updates land values. A HaussmannEpisode routes a boulevard, assembles land compulsorily, clears fabric, lays out a wider street with new plot frontages, updates land values with monumental effect.

The overlap between them is large — the difference is in parameters and in which primitives are chained in which order.

Composing Episodes into a Growth Sequence
A city's history is then a sequence of episodes applied to an initial state:

growCity(seed: FoundingConditions) -> CityState:
  s0 = initialise(seed)
  s1 = FoundingEpisode.run(s0, deriveFoundingParams(seed))
  s2 = OrganicGrowthEpisode.run(s1, {duration: 200, pressure: low})
  s3 = WallEpisode.run(s2, {era: medieval, reason: military})
  s4 = OrganicGrowthEpisode.run(s3, {duration: 150, pressure: medium})
  s5 = RailwayEpisode.run(s4, {era: 1840s, operator: private})
  s6 = SpeculativeDevelopmentEpisode.run(s5, {trigger: stationProximity})
  ...
  return sN
This is just function composition, which means you can:

Replay a city from any intermediate state
Branch at any point to explore counterfactual histories
Diff two states to understand what an episode changed
Validate each state independently against morphological rules
Parallelise independent episodes (two separate estate developments with no spatial overlap)
The Tricky Part: Organic Growth
Planned episodes are relatively clean functions — defined inputs, defined outputs, bounded spatial scope. Organic growth is harder because it's not a single agent making decisions; it's thousands of individual decisions accumulating over time.

One approach: model organic growth as an iterated micro-episode:

OrganicGrowthEpisode.run(state, params):
  repeat params.steps times:
    agent  = selectAgent(state.economy, state.ownership, params.era)
    action = selectAction(agent, state)  -- build, subdivide, amalgamate, change use
    target = selectTarget(agent, action, state)
    state  = applyAction(action, target, state)
  return state
Where selectAgent draws from a probability distribution over agent types (self-builder, speculative developer, institution, etc.) weighted by the current economy state, and selectAction reflects what that agent type would plausibly do given current land values, available plots, and era constraints.

This makes organic growth a special case of the same episode architecture — it just internally runs many small steps rather than one large transformation.

Parameter Derivation
One more layer worth thinking about: you probably don't want to set episode parameters manually for every episode in a sequence. Instead, parameters should be derived from context:

deriveEpisodeParams(episodeType, cityState, historicalContext):
  -- reads the current city state and the historical moment
  -- returns plausible parameters for that episode type
  -- can be randomised within historically coherent ranges
So deriveEpisodeParams(Railway, state, {era: 1840s, region: Britain}) would look at the city's existing infrastructure, find likely entry points, set operator = private, set landAssembly = compulsory (because UK Railway Clauses Act 1845), and randomise station count based on city size. The historical context encodes the "rules in vogue at this period" that you mentioned — rather than being baked into the episode itself, they're in the parameter derivation layer.

This keeps episodes themselves era-agnostic (a railway episode is a railway episode regardless of whether it's 1840s Britain or 1880s Argentina) while allowing the parameter derivation to encode the cultural and institutional specifics of time and place.

Summary of the Architecture
FoundingConditions
       ↓
   initialise()
       ↓
   CityState ←─────────────────────────────────────────┐
       ↓                                                │
EpisodeSequencer                                        │
  deriveNextEpisode(state, trajectory)                  │
       ↓                                                │
  EpisodeType + HistoricalContext                       │
       ↓                                                │
  deriveEpisodeParams(type, state, context)             │
       ↓                                                │
  EpisodeParams                                         │
       ↓                                                │
  episode.run(state, params)                            │
  [composed from Primitives]                            │
       ↓                                                │
  CityState' ─────────────────────────────────────────-┘
The episode loop runs until some termination condition (target population, target era, N steps). Each iteration the sequencer decides what happens next based on the current state — a city that just got a railway station has elevated probability of a speculative development episode near that station; a city under military threat has elevated probability of a fortification episode; a city with rising land values and fine-grain plots has elevated probability of amalgamation pressure.

The whole thing is inspectable at every step, replayable from any point, and extensible — adding a new episode type means implementing the primitives it composes and writing the parameter derivation logic, without touching anything else.

Era as a Filter on Episode Availability
The simplest version is just a predicate on each episode:

Episode {
  ...
  available: (Era, CityState) -> Bool
}
So RailwayEpisode.available returns true only if era >= industrial and the city has sufficient size/economic activity to justify a line. HaussmannBoulevardEpisode.available requires strong central planning authority and sufficient existing urban density to make widening meaningful.

But "available" is too binary. What you actually want is a probability weight that varies continuously with era and state:

Episode {
  ...
  weight: (Era, CityState) -> Float
}
This lets episodes fade in and fade out rather than switching on and off abruptly. The probability of a fortification episode doesn't drop to zero the moment gunpowder arrives — it declines gradually as the military logic of walls becomes obsolete, while star fort episodes briefly spike, then also decline.

Era as More Than a Timestamp
The temptation is to make Era just a year or a named period. But it's more useful as a bundle of capabilities and pressures:

Era {
  year:              Int
  
  -- technology available
  buildingMaterials: MaterialSet     -- timber, brick, iron, concrete, steel
  transportModes:    TransportSet    -- foot, horse, canal, rail, tram, car
  surveyCapability:  SurveyLevel     -- affects how regular planned layouts can be
  
  -- institutional context
  planningAuthority: AuthorityLevel  -- how much the state can impose on land
  tenureNorms:       TenureSystem    -- what ownership structures are normal
  financeCapacity:   FinanceLevel    -- how large a development can be funded
  
  -- pressures
  militaryThreat:    Float           -- drives fortification episodes
  tradePressure:     Float           -- drives port and market episodes  
  populationPressure: Float          -- drives density and expansion episodes
  sanitationCrisis:  Float           -- drives clearance and infrastructure episodes
}
Episodes then don't check the year directly — they check the relevant capability or pressure. RailwayEpisode checks transportModes.contains(Rail). HaussmannEpisode checks planningAuthority >= Strong && sanitationCrisis > threshold. WallEpisode checks militaryThreat > threshold && buildingMaterials.canBuildMasonry.

This means the same episode can recur in different historical contexts without being tied to a specific period, and the chronology engine can advance era by updating these fields rather than just incrementing a year counter.

The Chronology Engine
The tick loop then looks something like:

tick(state: CityState, era: Era) -> (CityState, Era):
  
  -- update era based on external events and city trajectory
  era' = advanceEra(era, state)
  
  -- score all episodes by weight given current era and state
  candidates = allEpisodes
    .filter(e => e.weight(era', state) > 0)
    .map(e => (e, e.weight(era', state)))
  
  -- select episode(s) — could be one, could be several concurrent
  selected = sample(candidates)
  
  -- derive parameters from context
  params = deriveParams(selected, era', state)
  
  -- run
  state' = selected.run(state, params)
  
  return (state', era')
advanceEra is interesting — it's not just the passage of time. It can be triggered by city state changes. A city that grows beyond a certain population density might trigger a sanitation crisis field increasing, which raises the weight of infrastructure episodes. A city that becomes a major port might develop stronger finance capacity, enabling larger estate development episodes. The era responds to the city as well as driving it.

Episode Interactions and Preconditions
Some episodes are only meaningful after others have occurred. You probably want a lightweight precondition system beyond just the weight:

RailwayEpisode {
  preconditions: [
    cityHas(InfrastructureType.Road),      -- needs existing street network
    eraHas(TransportMode.Rail),
    citySize > Size.Town
  ]
  
  -- preconditions gate availability entirely
  -- weight modulates probability given availability
  weight: (era, state) -> 
    baseWeight
    * economicActivityMultiplier(state)
    * regionRailDiffusionCurve(era)        -- railways spread from early-adopter regions
}
And some episodes unlock or increase the weight of others:

RailwayEpisode.onComplete(state) -> EpisodeWeightModifiers:
  return [
    increase(SpeculativeDevelopmentEpisode, near(state.newStations), factor: 3.0),
    increase(WarehouseDistrictEpisode, near(state.goodsYard), factor: 2.0),
    decrease(CanalEpisode, cityWide, factor: 0.3)   -- rail displaces canal investment
  ]
These modifiers are temporary boosts applied to the weight function for some number of subsequent ticks — they decay as the immediate effect of the episode fades.

Concurrent vs Sequential Episodes
Not all episodes need to be strictly sequential. Some happen simultaneously:

ConcurrencyRule:
  -- episodes with non-overlapping write sets can run concurrently
  -- episodes with overlapping spatial extents must be sequenced
  -- organic growth always runs concurrently with everything else
    (it's background process, not a discrete event)
So organic infill development keeps ticking in the background while a railway episode resolves. Two estate development episodes on opposite sides of the city can run in the same tick. A fortification episode and a port expansion episode might be concurrent if they affect different spatial areas.

What This Looks Like for a Specific City
A plausible chronology sequence for a northern European river-crossing town:

t=800   FoundingEpisode          {type: market, river_crossing}
t=800+  OrganicGrowth            {pressure: low, duration: ~100 ticks}
t=900   FortificationEpisode     {type: timber_palisade}
t=950   OrganicGrowth            {pressure: medium}
t=1050  FortificationEpisode     {type: stone_wall, replaces: timber_palisade}
t=1100  MarketEpisode            {charter_granted: true}  -- expands commercial zone
t=1100+ OrganicGrowth            {pressure: high, constrained_by: walls}
t=1200  SuburbEpisode            {outside_walls: true}    -- overflow beyond walls
t=1350  ShrinkageEpisode         {cause: plague}          -- population collapses
t=1350+ OrganicGrowth            {pressure: low}          -- slow recovery
t=1550  WallEpisode              {type: artillery_bastion} -- walls upgraded
t=1650  EstateEpisode            {agent: aristocratic, style: formal_garden}
t=1780  EstateEpisode            {agent: speculative, style: georgian_terrace}

The output of this sequence is a city with legible morphological layers — you can see the walls even though they're gone, you can see the railway cut, you can see the Georgian estate grid embedded in the organic medieval core.

The key property is that each episode only needs to know the current state — it doesn't need to know the history. The history is preserved in CityState.history for inspection and validation, but the episode logic itself is stateless with respect to past episodes. That's what makes the composition clean.

This is a genuinely interesting architecture because it uses the LLM for what it's actually good at — contextual reasoning over loosely structured state — while keeping the deterministic functions for what they're good at, which is spatial logic and structural consistency.

The Basic Shape
tick(state: CityState, era: Era) -> (CityState, Era):

  summary  = summarise(state, era)        -- compact representation for LLM
  decision = llm(summary, episodeMenu)    -- which episode(s), with what params
  parsed   = parseDecision(decision)      -- structured output
  
  validated = validate(parsed, state)     -- hard constraints checked deterministically
  
  state'   = parsed.episode.run(state, parsed.params)
  era'     = advanceEra(era, state')
  
  return (state', era')
The LLM sits in the decision loop but doesn't touch the spatial state directly. It reasons about what should happen; the functions determine what actually happens.

What the LLM Is Good at Here
The hard part of the chronology engine isn't "is a railway episode available" — that's a deterministic predicate. The hard part is the qualitative reasoning:

This city has been growing rapidly, has a congested medieval core, a new wealthy merchant class, and it's 1780. What would actually happen next?
There are three candidate estate development sites. Which one gets developed first, and in what style, given the city's social geography?
The city just had a plague episode. How does that change the character of the next few organic growth ticks?
These require synthesising multiple factors in a way that's hard to encode as explicit weighted rules. The LLM can reason across the whole state description and produce a decision that reflects genuine historical plausibility — because it has absorbed enormous amounts of urban history in training.

The Summarise Function
The LLM can't receive the raw spatial state — it's too large and the wrong format. You need a compact, legible summary:

summarise(state, era) -> String:

  return """
  City: [name], founded [era], [cultural_region]
  Current era: [year], [technology_level], [political_context]
  
  Morphological character:
    Core: [description of existing fabric layers]
    Recent growth: [last 3 episodes and their outcomes]
    Dominant plot grain: [fine/medium/coarse, with variation notes]
  
  Infrastructure:
    Street hierarchy: [description]
    Key facilities: [list of significant elements — walls, stations, markets]
  
  Economy:
    Primary activities: [list]
    Land value gradient: [description — where is valuable, where is cheap]
    Development pressure: [high/medium/low, where concentrated]
  
  Ownership landscape:
    Major unified holdings: [list with approximate size and owner type]
    Fragmentation level: [description]
  
  Current tensions:
    [automatically derived — eg "medieval core is congested",
     "railway station created pressure for warehousing to the east",
     "aristocratic estate to the north is decaying and may fragment"]
  
  Available episodes this era:
    [list with brief descriptions]
  """
The "tensions" field is particularly valuable — you can derive these deterministically from the state (congestion, land value gradients, ownership age, infrastructure gaps) and present them to the LLM as the problems the next episode might address. This grounds the LLM's reasoning in the actual spatial logic rather than letting it free-associate.

The Episode Menu
Rather than asking the LLM to invent episodes, you present it with the available typed episodes and ask it to select and parameterise:

episodeMenu(era, state) -> String:

  return """
  Available episodes:
  
  ORGANIC_GROWTH
    params: pressure (low/medium/high), spatial_focus (optional area)
    use when: background infill, no specific driver
  
  ESTATE_DEVELOPMENT
    params: agent_type, style, location, size
    use when: unified ownership opportunity, development pressure
    currently viable sites: [derived from ownership map]
  
  RAILWAY
    params: operator, entry_point, station_locations
    use when: sufficient city size, industrial era, economic pressure
    note: not yet present in this city
  
  CLEARANCE
    params: target_area, agent, replacement_type  
    use when: slum conditions, sanitation crisis, redevelopment pressure
    candidate areas: [derived from fabric age and condition]
  
  FORTIFICATION
    params: type, extent
    use when: military threat elevated
    current threat level: low — unlikely
  
  ... etc
  """
By presenting viable sites and current conditions alongside each episode type, you give the LLM the spatial grounding it needs to make a decision that's coherent with the actual city geography.

```
## Validation as a Hard Constraint Layer

The LLM will occasionally produce decisions that are spatially incoherent — placing an estate development on land that's already fully built, choosing a railway entry point that requires crossing a mountain, parameterising something outside valid ranges. Validation catches this before the episode runs:
```
validate(decision, state) -> Result<Decision, ValidationError>:
  
  check location exists and is buildable
  check agent type is plausible given ownership map
  check episode preconditions are met
  check params are within era-appropriate ranges
  
  if ValidationError:
    return error with explanation  -- feed back to LLM for retry
    -- or fall back to deterministic episode selection
```

The fallback matters — if the LLM produces something invalid after one retry, you don't want the whole tick to fail. Dropping back to the deterministic weighted selection for that tick is fine.

## A Richer Version: LLM as Urban Historian

You could push this further by giving the LLM a stronger persona — not just "select the next episode" but "reason as a urban historian about what would happen to this city":
```
system_prompt = """
You are reasoning about the historical development of a procedurally generated city.
Given the city's current state, select the most historically plausible next development episode.

Consider:
- What economic pressures are building?
- Which social groups have the capital and motivation to develop?
- What problems in the existing fabric demand a solution?
- What would a historian looking back at this city say happened next?

Your decision should feel like it emerged from the city's specific situation,
not like a generic application of period rules.
"""
This produces decisions that are sensitive to the city's particular character rather than just its era — a port city and an inland market town in the same era would get different decisions even with identical morphological states, because the LLM would reason about the different economic and social logic of each.

What This Architecture Buys You
Coherent narrative. The LLM naturally produces episodes that feel causally connected — each one responding to tensions created by the previous one. Without this, you risk a chronology that's historically plausible at each step but random in sequence.

Handling edge cases gracefully. Unusual combinations of founding conditions, unusual geographies, unusual growth trajectories — rather than requiring you to encode rules for every case, the LLM can reason about them using its general historical knowledge.

Legible history. The reasoning traces give you a human-readable account of the city's development that you can surface as in-game text, making the generated city feel like it has genuine depth.

Authorial control. You can steer the LLM by adjusting the summary, adding constraints, or seeding the prompt with specific historical themes — without touching the episode functions themselves. The spatial logic stays clean; the narrative logic is adjusted through the prompt layer.

The key discipline is maintaining the boundary: the LLM decides what and why; the functions decide how and where. If you let the LLM start reasoning about spatial geometry directly, you lose the structural guarantees that make the episode architecture valuable.

Why Bitmaps Are a Good Fit
The text summary approach requires you to decide upfront what to summarise — you're compressing the spatial state through a lossy function you designed. A vision-capable model looking at layer images can notice things you didn't think to summarise: an awkward gap between two development episodes, a plot grain that's unusually coarse for the era, a street pattern that suggests the city grew around an obstacle that's since been removed.

This is closer to how a urban morphologist actually reads a city — you look at the map and you see things. You don't read a structured description of it.

The Layer Stack
The useful layers are probably:

Spatial layers (rendered as images):
  street_network        -- line weight encodes hierarchy
  block_structure       -- filled polygons, colour encodes age/era
  plot_grain            -- fine lines showing plot boundaries
  building_footprints   -- filled, colour encodes use type
  land_values           -- heatmap, gradient surface
  ownership             -- colour encodes ownership type (unified/fragmented/institutional)
  terrain               -- elevation + water features
  infrastructure        -- railways, canals, walls, ports

Temporal layers:
  era_of_construction   -- each area coloured by when it was built
  episode_trace         -- which episode produced each spatial element
Each layer is a separate bitmap passed to the model. You don't need photorealistic rendering — schematic map-style rendering is probably better because it encodes exactly the information you want without visual noise.

What the Model Can Do With Them
Given this stack, a vision-capable model can reason about things that are genuinely hard to express as structured text:

Character reading. "The northwestern quarter has a coarser plot grain and more regular street pattern than the medieval core — this looks like a late 18th century estate development that was never fully built out. There are several large undeveloped plots remaining."

Tension identification. "The railway cut bisects what was previously a continuous fine-grain residential area. The eastern portion has been redeveloped around the goods yard; the western portion is unchanged and shows signs of decay relative to the rest of the city."

Opportunity spotting. "There's a unified institutional ownership block adjacent to the commercial core that's currently used as gardens. Given the land value gradient, this is the most likely site for amalgamation pressure."

Coherence checking. "The street pattern in the southern expansion doesn't connect well to the medieval core — there are only two crossing points, which is a bottleneck. This would historically have driven either a new street episode or concentration of development near those crossings."

These are qualitative spatial observations that would be extremely tedious to derive algorithmically but are natural outputs of visual reasoning.

The Revised Tick Loop
tick(state, era):

  -- render layer stack at current resolution
  layers = renderLayers(state, era)
  
  -- also generate the text context (era, available episodes, hard constraints)
  context = textContext(era, availableEpisodes, hardConstraints)
  
  -- multimodal call: images + text
  decision = llm(
    images: layers,
    text:   context,
    prompt: episodeSelectionPrompt
  )

  parsed    = parseDecision(decision)

The text context still carries things that don't render well — era capabilities, available episode types, hard constraints, the city's founding conditions. The images carry the spatial and morphological content. The model synthesises both.

Rendering Considerations
The renders need to be schematic enough to be readable at the scale you're working at. A few things matter:

Scale. You probably want two or three zoom levels — the whole city extent, the active growth frontier, and a detail view of any area of particular interest. A model looking only at the whole city can't read plot grain; a model looking only at a detail patch can't understand the city's overall structure.

Colour encoding. Era-of-construction encoded as a colour spectrum (cool colours for old, warm for recent) immediately communicates the morphological layering in a way that's visually readable. Ownership type as a distinct colour channel (institutional = purple, unified private = orange, fragmented = grey) immediately shows where amalgamation pressure exists.

Annotation. Sparse labelling — major landmarks, episode names on the areas they produced, key infrastructure — helps the model orient. Not so much text that it overwhelms the spatial content, but enough to anchor the reasoning.

Temporal sequence. You could pass not just the current state but the last N states as an animated sequence or a small multiples grid — showing how the city has changed over the last few ticks. This gives the model a sense of trajectory, not just current state, which is important for decisions like "this area has been declining for three episodes, which suggests a clearance episode is overdue."

A Deeper Possibility
Once you have this architecture, you can ask the model to do more than just select the next episode. You can ask it to:

Annotate the existing fabric. "Looking at this plot grain map, identify areas that appear morphologically anomalous relative to their surroundings." The output is a set of flagged regions that your validation system can then inspect more carefully.

Road ownership is one of the more interesting legal mechanisms in urban morphology because it's the point where private land becomes public infrastructure.

Who Owns Roads
In most legal systems, roads fall into a few distinct categories with different ownership structures:

Public highway / public right of way. The road surface and the airspace above it to a reasonable height are vested in the highway authority (state, municipality, crown depending on jurisdiction). Crucially, in English law the subsoil often remains with the adjacent landowner — they own down to the centreline of the road but can't do anything with it because the surface is a public right of way. This distinction matters for things like utility installation and tunnelling.

Adopted road. A road built by a private developer to a required standard and then formally handed over to the highway authority. This is the standard mechanism for new residential streets — the developer builds it, the council adopts it, the public maintains it thereafter. Before adoption, the road is private even if it looks public.

Unadopted / private road. Exists as a physical thing but has never been adopted. Maintenance falls to the adjacent landowners, usually in proportion to frontage. Common in older areas and on private estates. The road may be open to the public by custom without being formally public.

Estate road / internal road. Within a unified private landholding — a business park, a housing estate with a management company, a university campus — roads are private infrastructure owned by the landowner or a management entity. They may look like public streets but aren't.

How Subdivision Creates Roads
When a large plot is subdivided, new roads don't appear spontaneously — they're created through one of a few mechanisms, each with different ownership implications:

Dedication. The landowner formally dedicates a strip of their land as public highway. They surrender the surface rights; the highway authority accepts maintenance responsibility. The subsoil typically remains with the original owner. This is how most Georgian and Victorian street layouts were created — the estate owner laid out streets and dedicated them, retaining the plots on either side.

Compulsory acquisition. The state forces the landowner to surrender land for a road at a fixed price. Used for major infrastructure, road widening, new arterial routes. The state acquires full ownership of the strip, not just surface rights.

Implied dedication. A landowner allows the public to use a route for long enough that public right of way is established by custom, without any formal legal act. Common with old lanes and footpaths that predate formal road law.

Developer agreement. In modern planning, a developer seeking permission to subdivide a large plot must typically enter a legal agreement committing to build roads to a specified standard and then dedicate or offer them for adoption. The roads don't legally become public until adoption, but the mechanism is contractually committed before any building happens.

The Geometry of Road Creation in Subdivision
When a large plot is subdivided and a new road runs through it, what actually happens spatially is:

The original plot boundary stays legally significant — it's now the outer boundary of the development. The road strip is carved out of the plot as a distinct legal entity, and the remaining land on either side becomes new plots with frontage onto the new road.

This is why you get that characteristic pattern in estate developments where all the plots on a new street have a consistent setback from the road — the road strip was defined first, then the plots were measured back from it. The road is not a gap between plots; it's a positively defined strip that the plots are organised around.

In practice the geometry looks like:

Original large plot boundary
├── Road strip (dedicated / to be adopted)
│   ├── Plot A (fronting road, left side)
│   ├── Plot B
│   ├── Plot C
│   └── ...
└── Plot D (rear, if block has depth)
The road strip has a defined width — in English Victorian development typically 40–60 feet including pavement — and the plots are measured from the back of the pavement, not from the road centreline.

For Your Generator
The implication for the subdivision primitive is that road creation is part of plot subdivision, not a separate step. When an estate episode subdivides a large plot:

The episode lays out the road network first — this defines the skeleton of the new development
Road strips are carved from the plot as dedicated or to-be-adopted strips, changing their ownership to public/highway-authority
The remaining land is then subdivided into plots fronting those roads
The original plot boundary becomes the development boundary, which may or may not align with block boundaries in the wider city
For organic subdivision — a single plot being split into two — there's usually no new road created. The existing road provides frontage for both new plots, which is why organic subdivision almost always produces plots that are deeper relative to their width, both halves sharing the original street frontage.

New roads only appear in organic growth when a plot is large enough that the rear portion has no road access — then a new lane or mews is created to serve the back land. This is the origin of most of the secondary lanes and mews in organic urban fabric — they're access solutions to landlocked rear plots, not planned infrastructure.

So the rule of thumb is: planned subdivision creates roads first then plots; organic subdivision creates plots first and roads only when access demands it. The morphological signature of each is distinct and visible in the grain of the resulting fabric.

Exactly — and this is one of the most generative tensions in urban morphology because it produces recognisable patterns that vary systematically with landowner type and era.

The Basic Tradeoff
A landowner with a large plot can extract value from it in two ways:

Depth strategy — minimise road, maximise plot area. Build a single road along one edge, sell or develop deep plots behind it. You lose little land to road but the rear plots are less valuable because they're further from the street. Works when land is cheap and plot depth doesn't significantly discount value.

Frontage strategy — maximise road, accept more land lost to infrastructure. A grid of streets produces many plots each with good frontage. You lose 15–25% of the land area to roads but every plot commands a frontage premium. Works when land is expensive and frontage is the primary value driver.

The crossover point between these strategies depends on land value, era, and what's being built. Victorian residential estates in growing cities almost always chose the frontage strategy because the frontage premium was large. Industrial estates chose the depth strategy because factories don't need street frontage the way shops and houses do.

How This Produces Recognisable Patterns
The mews pattern. A landowner wants to maximise frontage plots on the main street but also extract value from the rear land. Solution: main street plots with houses, a narrow mews lane running parallel behind them serving a second row of smaller plots (originally stables and coach houses, later converted to housing). You get two tiers of value from one block — high-value frontage and lower-value rear — at the cost of one narrow lane. London is full of this.

The back-land problem. A landowner is too conservative with roads early on — lays out one street frontage, sells those plots, and is left with a large landlocked rear parcel. Now they need to create a new road to access it, but that road has to cross land they've already sold, which requires buying back access or negotiating an easement. This is expensive and often doesn't happen, which is why you get those characteristic pockets of underdeveloped backland in otherwise dense urban fabric.

The cul-de-sac compromise. A landowner wants to minimise road area but needs to serve plots that can't front onto the main street. A cul-de-sac serves the maximum number of plots for the minimum road length — but produces plots with low permeability and reduces the value of the served plots relative to through-street frontage. Common in speculative suburban development where the developer wants to maximise plot count while minimising infrastructure cost.

The grid premium. When a landowner commits to a full grid layout — accepting 20%+ of the area going to roads — they get something back: every plot is a corner plot or near one, values are more uniform across the development, and the estate is more attractive to buyers because permeability is high. The Grosvenor estate's commitment to a full grid in Belgravia is partly why it commanded premium prices relative to less permeable nearby developments.

Variation by Landowner Type
Different landowner types make this tradeoff differently in ways that produce morphologically distinct outcomes:

Aristocratic estate with long time horizon. Can afford to invest in roads because they're not selling the freehold — they retain the reversionary interest on leases. Road investment increases rental income across the whole estate, which compounds over decades. Tends toward generous road provision and full grids. Grosvenor, Bedford, Portland estates all exhibit this.

Speculative developer with short time horizon. Wants to maximise sellable plot area and minimise infrastructure cost before moving on. Tends toward minimal road provision — one or two access streets, deep plots, cul-de-sacs. The roads are built to the minimum standard that will get planning permission and then offered for adoption.

Self-builder / smallholder. Builds for their own use, may never subdivide. Typically no road creation at all — just uses existing access. When subdivision eventually happens it's piecemeal and access is improvised, which is why informal settlements develop such irregular lane networks.

Institutional landowner. Optimises for the institution's specific needs rather than market value. A university creates paths and roads where pedestrian movement between buildings demands it, not where market value would be maximised. A railway company creates roads only where goods movement requires them. The road network reflects operational logic rather than land value logic.

Municipal / state developer. Can internalise the road cost because they're also the road authority. Tends toward generous road provision because the cost is socialised — but also tends toward over-engineering roads because the highway department has standards to meet regardless of whether the road serves enough plots to justify the width.

The Timing Problem
There's a further tension around when roads are built relative to when plots are sold or developed.

Roads first — the landowner builds all the roads before selling any plots. Maximum infrastructure cost upfront, but all plots are immediately serviced and sellable. The whole development is legible from day one. Risk: the landowner is exposed to the full infrastructure cost before receiving any revenue. If the market moves against them, they've built roads to nowhere.

Roads as needed — the landowner builds roads only as plots are sold, extending the network incrementally. Minimises upfront cost but produces an incremental, sometimes incoherent layout. The road network reflects the sequence of sales rather than any geometric plan. Risk: early plots may be poorly connected to later ones; the overall layout may be inefficient.

Roads promised but not built — the landowner sells plots on the promise that roads will be built later. Common in speculative booms, produces the characteristic pattern of plotted but unbuilt streets that you see on old maps of expanding cities. Some of these plots were never developed and the promised streets never built, leaving ghost street patterns detectable in property boundaries long after.

For the Generator
This tension is a direct input to the road layout primitive. When an estate episode runs, the road generation should be parameterised by:

RoadStrategy {
  frontageOptimisation:  Float    -- 0 = depth strategy, 1 = frontage strategy
  infrastructureBudget:  Float    -- affects road width and build quality
  timeHorizon:           Horizon  -- short/long affects upfront vs incremental
  accessRequirement:     Use      -- residential/commercial/industrial
                                     determines minimum access standard
}
And this strategy should be derived from the agent type doing the development — an aristocratic estate gets high frontage optimisation and long time horizon; a speculative developer gets low infrastructure budget and short time horizon. The morphological outcome of each is different and recognisable, which is exactly the kind of character variation you want the episode system to produce.

How They Fit Into Planning
The planning treatment of land uses has changed dramatically over time, which is itself a generator input.

Pre-modern (before ~1850). No formal zoning. Uses mixed freely at the plot level — a butcher's shop below, housing above, a tannery next door. The only separations were driven by nuisance (smelly or noisy uses pushed to edges by social pressure and market forces) and by institutional land ownership (church precincts, guild halls, market rights). The market square and the church were typically the only uses that commanded dedicated large plots with civic intentionality.

19th century sanitary reform. The first wave of planning intervention was driven by public health. Noxious industries (slaughterhouses, tanneries, gasworks) were pushed to the urban fringe by bylaw and by disgust. The clearance episode you'd model for this era isn't about land value — it's about miasma theory and cholera. Parks emerge as a deliberate planning intervention (Olmsted, Paxton) — not organic like a market square but imposed as civic infrastructure.

Early 20th century zoning. Use-separation becomes doctrine. The 1916 New York zoning resolution is the canonical first modern zoning code — separating residential, commercial, and industrial uses into distinct zones. This is where the idea that uses shouldn't mix becomes encoded in law. Produces the pure-use districts that dominate American urbanism.

Mid 20th century modernist planning. Use separation taken to its logical extreme. Residential superblocks, commercial centres, industrial estates — all separated and connected by roads. The street as mixed-use social space is explicitly rejected. Retail is concentrated into planned centres (shopping precincts, malls). This is the era of the supermarket, the business park, the out-of-town retail park.

Late 20th / 21st century mixed-use return. Reaction against separation. Planning policy in most Western cities now actively encourages mixed use, ground-floor activation, walkability. The supermarket is increasingly politically disfavoured in city centres; street-front retail is protected; residential above commercial is incentivised. New large uses (arenas, hospitals, universities) are expected to integrate with their surroundings rather than forming enclaves.

Plot Logic by Use Type
Different uses have characteristic plot requirements that constrain where they can go and what they produce morphologically:

Frontage-dependent uses — retail shops, restaurants, banks — need to be visible and accessible from the street. They colonise ground floors and drive narrow-frontage, shallow-depth plot patterns. They cluster because footfall is mutually reinforcing.

Footprint-dependent uses — supermarkets, cinemas, sports halls — need a minimum continuous floor area that typically exceeds what a single plot can provide. They require amalgamation and are therefore rare in fine-grain fabric. They tend to appear on cleared sites, edge locations, or purpose-built centres.

Precinct-forming uses — cathedrals, hospitals, universities, railway stations — generate their own surrounding land use logic. A cathedral creates a close; a hospital creates a cluster of ancillary uses (pharmacies, specialist suppliers, staff housing); a university creates student housing, cafes, bookshops in the surrounding streets. These are uses that don't just occupy a plot — they reorganise the surrounding district.

Permanent uses — cemeteries, parks, major civic buildings — once established are almost never redeveloped. They become fixed points around which everything else organises. In your generator these should probably set a flag indicating near-zero probability of clearance or redevelopment episodes.

Transitional uses — car parks, surface storage, temporary markets — occupy land that's waiting for something better. High land values suppress them; low land values allow them. They're a useful signal in the generator: a district with many transitional uses is one where development pressure hasn't yet arrived or has stalled.

The Mixed-Use Dimension
One thing worth modelling explicitly is the vertical dimension of mixed use. In most pre-modern and contemporary urban fabric, a single plot carries multiple uses stacked vertically:

Ground floor:   retail / workshop / commercial
First floor:    office / storage / workshop
Upper floors:   residential
Rear / yard:    storage / workshop / garden
This is the default condition of the pre-zoning city and is returning as the default aspiration of contemporary planning. The plot use isn't a single value — it's a profile across floors and parts of the plot.

For the generator this suggests that use on a plot shouldn't be a single enum but a structure that can carry mixed use — with the ground floor use and the upper floor use potentially different, and the planning era determining how likely that mixture is.

Likely to have a complex, irregular core with legible ancient geometry only in fragments — a forum that became a market square, a Roman street still followed by a modern road. Examples: Rome, London, Istanbul, Xi'an.

Medieval (500–1400 AD). Most European and Middle Eastern historic cities. Founded for defence, trade, or religious reasons. Organic core with walls (possibly multiple rings as the city grew). Fine grain plot structure in the core. Multiple legible layers from medieval through to industrial. Examples: most English county towns, Prague, Bruges, Fez.

Early modern (1400–1700 AD). Renaissance and Baroque foundations, colonial foundations in the Americas and Asia. More planned from the start — grid or radial layouts are common. Often founded by a specific political act rather than organic growth. Examples: Valletta, many Spanish colonial cities, St Petersburg (early 1700s).

Industrial (1700–1900 AD). Founded or massively expanded by industrial activity — mining towns, mill towns, railway junctions, port expansions. Often have a thin or absent pre-industrial layer. The railway is typically present from early in the city's history rather than being a later imposition. Examples: Manchester, Pittsburgh, many Midlands towns.

Modern (1900–1960 AD). Planned new towns, colonial administrative capitals, cities founded by specific infrastructure projects. Often have a strong planning intention from the start — garden city layouts, modernist grids, colonial civic centres. Examples: Canberra, Chandigarh, Brasília, many Soviet new towns.

Recent (post-1960 AD). Fast-growing cities in the Global South, new economic zones, satellite towns. Often informal in origin with formal planning imposed retrospectively, or entirely planned but developing rapidly beyond the plan. Very thin morphological layering — sometimes only one or two episodes deep. Examples: Shenzhen, many African secondary cities.

How Age Interacts With the Episode Sequence
Age determines the starting point of the chronology loop and therefore which episodes are in the history by the time the city reaches its current state.

An ancient city might have run something like:

Roman founding episode        -- grid, forum, walls
Abandonment episode           -- partial depopulation, fabric decay
Medieval resettlement         -- organic growth over Roman bones
Multiple wall episodes        -- expanding rings
Medieval organic growth       -- fine grain accumulation
Plague shrinkage
Renaissance replanning        -- some streets widened, piazzas created
Industrial expansion          -- railway, factories, worker housing
Sanitation episode            -- sewers, clearance of worst slums
20th century clearance        -- some modernist intervention
Contemporary infill
A modern city might have run:

Planned founding episode      -- grid, civic centre
Rapid organic growth          -- informal settlement at edges
Infrastructure episode        -- roads, utilities catching up with growth
Contemporary densification
The difference in morphological complexity between these is enormous — and it's directly a function of age.

Age as a Generator Parameter
In practice, age probably belongs in the founding conditions bundle alongside geography and cultural region:

FoundingConditions {
  geography:      GeographicContext
  foundingEra:    Era               -- sets the starting point
  foundingType:   FoundingType      -- organic, planned, colonial, industrial etc
  culturalRegion: CulturalRegion
  ageClass:       AgeClass          -- determines episode history depth
}
From ageClass and foundingEra you can derive a probabilistic episode history — not a deterministic sequence but a plausible set of layers with appropriate uncertainty about which specific episodes ran and how strongly. An ancient city definitely has a medieval layer but you can randomise whether it has a Renaissance replanning episode or went straight from medieval to industrial.

The episode history then drives the initial city state that the live simulation or generation runs from — rather than simulating every episode from 800 AD in full detail, you can bootstrap a plausible current state from the age class and let the detailed simulation run only for the most recent few episodes where the morphological detail matters for gameplay.

The Main Categories
Trade and Commerce The most common founding purpose historically. A city exists because goods move through it or are exchanged there.

River crossing town — exists because a ford or bridge made it the natural crossing point on a trade route. The bridge and the market are the twin generators of the whole layout. Examples: Oxford, Frankfurt, countless English market towns.
Port — exists because of sheltered water access. The waterfront is the primary value axis; everything else organises behind it. Warehouse districts, merchant quarters, customs houses cluster at the water's edge. Examples: Venice, Lisbon, Rotterdam, Hamburg.
Canal town — similar to port logic but linear rather than nodal. The canal is the spine; wharves and warehouses line it; the town develops perpendicular to the water.
Market town — exists to serve a rural hinterland as a collection and distribution point. The market square is the spatial generator. Typically modest in size, regular in form, with a strong weekly rhythm that shaped its layout.
Entrepôt — exists purely as a transshipment point between two trading systems. Minimal production; maximum storage and finance. Singapore, Hong Kong, historical Bruges. Warehouse and financial districts dominate.
Military and Defensive

Fortress town — founded to control a strategic point. The fortification is the primary spatial generator; everything else is subordinate to defensive logic. Street layouts often reflect sight lines and fields of fire rather than movement efficiency. Examples: Carcassonne, many bastide towns, colonial presidios.
Garrison town — exists to house and supply a standing army. Barracks, parade grounds, and officers' quarters are the dominant uses. Often has an unusually regular layout because military planners imposed order. Examples: Colchester, many British colonial cantonment towns.
Border town — exists at the boundary between two political entities. Often has a dual character reflecting both sides of the border — different architectural traditions, different street patterns on each side of a historical divide.
Naval base — like a port but militarised. Dockyards, rope walks, naval hospitals, officers' quarters. Examples: Portsmouth, Brest, Cartagena.
Religious

Pilgrimage city — exists because it contains a sacred site. The shrine or cathedral is the spatial generator; everything else serves the pilgrim economy. Hostels, markets selling devotional goods, processional routes. Examples: Santiago de Compostela, Lourdes, Varanasi, Mecca.
Monastic foundation — a city that grew around a monastery or cathedral. The religious precinct predates the urban fabric and often dominates it spatially. Examples: Bury St Edmunds, Canterbury, many Irish towns.
Sacred geography — cities founded on cosmologically significant sites — river confluences, mountain bases, alignment points. Particularly common in South and East Asian urbanism. The spatial logic reflects ritual requirements rather than economic ones.
Administrative and Political

Capital city — exists to house the apparatus of government. Civic institutions, courts, ministries, palaces are the dominant uses. Often has a monumental spatial logic — wide boulevards, large civic squares, formal axes — because political power needs to be expressed spatially. Examples: Washington DC, Canberra, Brasília, Versailles.
Colonial administrative centre — founded to administer a colonial territory. Has a distinctive dual structure: a formal European quarter with grid layout and civic buildings, and an indigenous quarter with different morphological logic. Examples: Delhi (New vs Old), Nairobi, Dakar.
Regional administrative town — exists as the seat of regional government. County town, prefecture capital. Courthouse, administrative offices, and professional services are the dominant uses alongside residential.
University town — the university is the primary institution and employer. The city exists in a dependent relationship with the institution. Examples: Oxford, Cambridge, Heidelberg, many American college towns.
Industrial and Extractive

Mining town — exists to extract a specific resource. Often in geographically constrained locations (valleys, hillsides) that produce distinctive irregular layouts. Can collapse entirely when the resource is exhausted. Examples: many Welsh valley towns, Colorado mining towns, Potosí.
Mill town — exists because water power or later steam power made manufacturing viable there. The mill or factory is the spatial generator; worker housing clusters around it. Often has a strong linear logic following the watercourse. Examples: Manchester, Lowell, many Pennine towns.
Company town — purpose-built by a single industrial employer to house its workforce. Extremely regular, often paternalistic in layout — employer controls all land and uses. Examples: Saltaire, Port Sunlight, Pullman (Chicago), many Soviet industrial cities.
Processing town — exists to process agricultural output (milling, brewing, tanning, textile finishing). Often at the boundary between agricultural hinterland and trading network. Smelly or noxious industries drive specific spatial patterns.
Transport and Infrastructure

Railway town — either created by or massively transformed by the railway. The station and goods yard are the spatial generators. Examples: Crewe, Swindon (the works town), many American railroad towns.
Road junction town — exists because major routes cross there. The coaching inn, the market, and the crossroads are the spatial generators. Superseded by motorway junctions in the modern era, which produce a different kind of settlement (service stations, distribution centres, hotels).
Canal junction — exists where canal routes met, requiring maintenance facilities, warehousing, and worker housing.
Airport city / aerotropolis — the newest version of this type. Exists to serve an airport. Hotels, logistics, business parks organised around the terminal and runway.
Residential and Amenity

Spa and resort town — exists because of a natural amenity (thermal springs, seaside, mountain scenery). The leisure infrastructure is the spatial generator — pump rooms, promenades, hotels, assembly rooms. Often has an unusually high-quality built environment because wealthy visitors demanded it. Examples: Bath, Baden-Baden, Brighton.
Commuter town — exists as a dormitory for a larger nearby city. Minimal employment base; the railway or road connection to the metropolis is the primary spatial generator. Often has a thin commercial centre and an extensive residential hinterland.
Retirement town — similar to resort logic but residential rather than transient. Amenity-focused uses dominate; employment base is service-oriented.
Garden city / new town — purpose-built as a planned residential alternative to the industrial city. Strong planning intention; often mixed use by design; frequently has an unusually complete civic infrastructure because it was planned rather than accumulated. Examples: Letchworth, Milton Keynes, Vällingby.
How Founding Purpose Shapes Spatial Logic
Each founding purpose produces a characteristic spatial signature:

Primary axis. A port organises perpendicular to the waterfront. A market town organises around the market square. A garrison town organises around the parade ground. A pilgrimage city organises along the processional route to the shrine. The primary axis is the highest-value land and the strongest attractor for secondary uses.

Dominant plot type. A warehouse district has large, deep plots with loading access. A market town has narrow frontage plots facing the square. A company town has uniform residential plots of identical size. The dominant plot type reflects the dominant use.

Growth direction. A port grows away from the water as land values behind the waterfront develop. A market town grows along its road approaches. A mining town grows along the valley or toward the seam. The founding purpose constrains the direction of least resistance for expansion.

Vulnerability and resilience. A city founded for a single purpose is vulnerable to that purpose becoming obsolete. Mining towns collapse when the seam is exhausted. Railway towns decline when the works close. Port towns decay when shipping routes change. A city with multiple founding purposes — a port that's also a market town and a regional capital — is more resilient because no single change can eliminate all its reasons to exist.

For the Generator
Founding purpose probably belongs alongside age and geographic context in the founding conditions bundle, and it should drive several downstream parameters:

FoundingPurpose {
  primaryPurpose:    PurposeType        -- the dominant reason the city exists
  secondaryPurposes: PurposeType[]      -- additional reasons (most real cities have several)
  spatialGenerator:  SpatialElement     -- the primary attractor (waterfront, market square,
                                           fortress, shrine, station)
  dominantUse:       UseType            -- what occupies the most land and generates most value
  growthAxis:        GrowthDirection    -- which direction expansion naturally flows
  vulnerabilities:   Vulnerability[]    -- what changes could cause decline episodes
}
The spatialGenerator is particularly important — it's the fixed point around which the founding episode organises the initial layout, and it persists as an attractor through all subsequent growth episodes even as the city changes character. The Roman forum becomes the medieval market square becomes the modern civic plaza — the use changes but the spatial logic of centrality persists.

The vulnerabilities parameter enables decline and transformation episodes — a port town that loses its shipping routes should have elevated probability of a decay episode followed eventually by either a regeneration episode (converting docks to housing and culture, as in London's Docklands) or a shrinkage episode (population loss, abandonment).

This is a really clean problem to frame. You have three distinct sources of a city's character:

Endogenous geography — things visible on your regional map. The river, the harbour shape, the valley, the defendable hill, the flat plain suitable for a grid. Your generator already produces these and they correctly drive settlement placement and basic city type.

Regional context — things visible if you zoomed out one level. Whether this river connects to a larger trading network, whether this harbour faces a busy sea lane, whether this road connects two larger cities or dead-ends in wilderness. Partially inferable from your map if you model the region as a fragment of something larger.

Exogenous world state — things that are fundamentally off-map. Whether there's a colonial power that needs administrative centres, whether an industrial revolution is happening somewhere that creates demand for raw materials, whether a religious movement generates pilgrimage traffic, whether a railway company has capital to invest. These cannot be derived from geography at all — they're historical accidents that arrive from outside.

The Key Distinction
The question is which of these you simulate and which you randomise. I'd argue:

Simulate anything that has a spatial correlate on your map — because the simulation gives you coherent relationships between settlements. If two cities are both on the same river, their trading relationship is a simulation output not a random variable. If a road connects three towns, the middle one benefits from through traffic in a way that's derivable.

Randomise anything that arrives from outside the map as an external stimulus — because there's no spatial logic to derive it from. You're essentially sampling from a distribution of "what kinds of external pressure arrive at settlements of this type in this era."

What Needs to Be Randomised
Grouping the exogenous variables by category:

Trade route status Whether your harbour is on a busy international sea lane or a backwater is fundamentally a function of world geography you haven't modelled. You can randomise this as a single parameter — something like tradeRouteSignificance: none/local/regional/international — and let it drive port size, merchant wealth, warehouse district scale, and cosmopolitan character. The geography tells you whether a harbour exists; the randomised parameter tells you how important it is to the wider world.

Resource demand Whether your coal seam or iron ore deposit gets exploited depends on whether there's industrial demand for it somewhere in the world. The deposit is endogenous (your geology layer produces it); the demand is exogenous. Randomise as an industrial demand signal that arrives at a particular era and drives whether an extractive episode fires.

Colonial or imperial attention Whether a settlement gets chosen as a colonial administrative centre, a missionary base, or a garrison town for an imperial power is entirely exogenous. You can randomise this as an event that arrives with some probability during the relevant era — a colonialDesignation event that triggers a planned replanning episode and imposes a different morphological logic on part of the city.

Religious significance Whether a local shrine becomes a major pilgrimage destination is partly endogenous (a significant geographical feature, an old sacred site) but mostly exogenous (a miracle is reported, a saint is buried there, a religious order decides to build there). Randomise as a religiousSignificanceEvent that can fire at any era and drives shrine/cathedral episodes.

Railway investment decisions Whether a railway reaches your city and whether it's a through station or a terminus depends on decisions made by railway companies responding to economic geography well beyond your map. You know the terrain (which constrains route options endogenously) but not the investment decision. Randomise as a railwayDesignation: none/branch/through/junction/terminus that arrives in the industrial era with probabilities weighted by settlement size and position on road network.

Political designation Whether a settlement becomes a regional capital, a county town, a bishopric is a political decision made by actors outside the map. Randomise as a politicalDesignation event that fires with probability weighted by settlement size and centrality, and drives civic building episodes.

Catastrophic external events Plague, war, fire, flood — some of these are endogenous (a flood is more likely in a flood plain your map shows) but many arrive from outside. A plague is a regional or global event; a war is a political event. Randomise as era-weighted external shocks that hit settlements with varying severity.

A Practical Architecture
Rather than randomising these independently, you could model them as an offmap world state — a simple set of parameters that represent the wider world your region sits in:

WorldState {
  era:                  Era
  dominantSeaPower:     SeaPower?       -- drives colonial events
  tradeRouteMap:        RouteSignificance  -- which coasts/rivers are busy
  industrialDemand:     ResourceDemand[]   -- which resources the world wants
  religiousMovements:   ReligiousEvent[]   -- active pilgrimage traditions
  railwayInvestment:    InvestmentLevel    -- capital available for rail
  politicalStructure:   PoliticalContext   -- empire, nation states, fragmented
}
This world state is randomised once at generation time (or authored for a specific historical flavour) and then drives the probability of external events arriving at each settlement over the chronology. It's not a full world simulation — it's a compact parameterisation of the off-map context that makes the on-map results coherent.

The key property is that world state is shared across all settlements in the region. If railwayInvestment is high, multiple settlements get railway episodes — but which ones, and in what order, depends on their endogenous geography and size. The external stimulus is common; the response is spatially differentiated. That's what produces the realistic pattern of railway development spreading across a region rather than each city independently rolling dice.

The Boundary Cases
Some things sit awkwardly between endogenous and exogenous:

Market town status — whether a settlement becomes the dominant market town for its hinterland is partly geographic (central location, good roads) and partly political (who gets the market charter). You can derive a candidate from geography and then randomise whether the charter arrives.

Road connections to off-map destinations — your anchor roads run to the map edge. What's beyond the edge matters: a road to a major off-map city generates through traffic; a road to wilderness generates nothing. You can randomise the off-map endpoint significance as a simple parameter on each road that exits the map boundary.

River trade — a navigable river is endogenous; whether it connects to a major trading network off-map is exogenous. Same treatment as harbour significance.

These boundary cases are probably best handled by generating a simple map edge context — for each point where infrastructure exits your map, assign a significance value that represents what's on the other side. This gives you the minimum off-map information needed to make on-map development coherent without simulating the whole world.

Yes — weather is a good example of something that's partly endogenous (your terrain generates rain shadows, coastal moderating effects, frost hollows) and partly needs a baseline that comes from where the region sits in the wider world.

The Two Components
Macroclimate is exogenous — it's where your region sits on the planet. Latitude drives temperature range and season length. Continental vs coastal position drives humidity and rainfall distribution. Prevailing wind direction drives which slopes are wet and which are dry. You can't derive any of this from your regional terrain — it has to be set as a founding parameter.

Mesoclimate and microclimate are endogenous — they're what your terrain does to the macroclimate. A mountain range forces air upward, causing orographic rainfall on the windward side and a rain shadow on the leeward. A valley bottom collects cold air at night, producing frost hollows. A south-facing slope in the northern hemisphere is warmer and drier than a north-facing one. Coastal proximity moderates temperature extremes. These are all derivable from your terrain if you know the macroclimate baseline.

What Macroclimate Parameters You Need
A fairly small set covers most of the variation that matters for city generation:

Macroclimate {
  latitude:           Float           -- drives temperature and season length
  continentality:     Float           -- 0 = oceanic, 1 = continental
                                         drives temperature range and humidity
  prevailingWind:     Direction        -- drives orographic effects
  baseTemperature:    TempRange        -- annual min/max before terrain modification
  baseRainfall:       RainfallPattern  -- seasonal distribution
  extremeEvents:      HazardProfile    -- hurricane, monsoon, drought, freeze
}
From these plus terrain you can derive most of what you need for city generation purposes — you don't need a full climate simulation, just enough to make the downstream effects coherent.

How Weather Drives City Generation
Weather affects the city generator at several levels, some obvious and some subtle.

Founding location — already partly in your pipeline. Harbours need to be sheltered from prevailing storms. River crossings need to be above flood plain. Settlements avoid frost hollows and exposed ridgelines. But the prevailing wind direction also matters: medieval cities typically placed noxious industries (tanneries, slaughterhouses, gasworks) downwind of the residential core. If you know the prevailing wind you can derive which side of the city industry colonises.

Building form — climate drives whether buildings face inward (hot dry climates, where the courtyard provides shade and the street is hostile) or outward (temperate climates, where the street is social space and sunlight is welcome). It drives roof pitch (steep roofs in high snowfall areas, flat roofs in dry climates), wall thickness, window size, and arcade provision. These aren't just aesthetic — they're constraints on plot coverage, building depth, and street relationship that propagate through the whole morphological logic.

Street orientation — in hot climates streets are often oriented to maximise shade, running perpendicular to the sun's path and narrow enough that buildings shade each other. In cold climates streets are sometimes oriented to maximise solar gain. In windy climates streets avoid channelling prevailing winds. The Spanish colonial Laws of the Indies actually specified street orientation relative to prevailing winds — a rare case of climate being encoded directly in planning law.

Seasonal economy — a city whose primary purpose is seasonally driven (a fishing port, an agricultural market town, a ski resort) has a different spatial logic from one with year-round activity. Seasonal uses produce specific building types (fish processing sheds, grain stores, seasonal hotels) and specific patterns of use intensity that affect which parts of the city stay active and which go dormant.

Flood and fire risk — both are partly climate-driven. High rainfall regions produce flood risk that drives settlement away from river banks and eventually produces embankment and flood defence episodes. Dry hot climates produce fire risk that drives building material choices (masonry over timber) and influences plot coverage limits. A city that has burned repeatedly has a different morphological character from one that hasn't — each fire is a clearance episode that resets part of the fabric, which is why cities like London (1666) and Chicago (1871) have unusually regular street patterns in their cores relative to their age.

Agricultural hinterland — the weather determines what the surrounding region produces, which determines what the city processes and trades. A wet temperate climate produces grain and wool; a Mediterranean climate produces wine and olive oil; a tropical coastal climate produces spices and sugar. This drives which processing and warehouse uses dominate, which in turn drives plot sizes and industrial district character.

Weather as an Episode Trigger
Beyond the founding effects, weather drives episodic events in the chronology:

WeatherEpisodes:
  FloodEpisode         -- damages low-lying fabric, drives embankment construction
  FireEpisode          -- clears fabric, probability weighted by building material
                          and drought conditions
  StormEpisode         -- damages port infrastructure, drives harbour improvement
  DroughtEpisode       -- drives water infrastructure investment
  HarshWinterEpisode   -- accelerates building decay, drives rebuilding
These are different from the development episodes in that they're destructive rather than constructive — they clear fabric rather than create it. But they feed the same episode architecture: a flood episode clears plots in the flood plain, updates land values downward in that area, and increases the probability of a flood defence episode in subsequent ticks.

The probability of each weather episode should be derived from the macroclimate parameters plus the terrain — a city on a floodplain in a high-rainfall continental climate has high flood episode probability; a city on a hillside in a maritime climate has low flood probability but higher storm probability if it's also coastal.

The Practical Upshot
For the generator, weather probably needs two treatments:

At founding — macroclimate sets a handful of parameters that modify the founding episode logic: building orientation rules, street width norms, dominant construction material, which side of the city noxious uses go, whether a courtyard typology or a street-facing typology dominates.

In the chronology loop — weather events fire probabilistically based on climate profile and terrain, acting as destructive episodes that interact with the development episode sequence. A city that burns three times in its medieval period looks very different from one that doesn't, and that difference should be derivable from its climate rather than random.

The macroclimate parameters themselves are part of the world state bundle alongside trade route significance and political context — things that are set once at generation time and shared across all settlements in the region, producing coherent regional variation rather than each city independently rolling its own climate dice.

Agriculture as the Foundation Layer
Before a city can exist, its hinterland has to be able to feed it. The agricultural productivity of the surrounding region sets a ceiling on how large a settlement can grow before it needs to import food — which then requires trade infrastructure, which requires wealth, which drives the commercial episodes that build the city.

This makes agriculture not just a land use category but a precondition for urbanisation that sits below everything else in the pipeline. Your existing layer stack probably needs it explicitly:

Soil formation is derivable from geology, terrain, climate, and hydrology — alluvial river valleys produce deep fertile soils, thin upland soils form on exposed rock, waterlogged lowlands produce peat, arid areas produce poor soils unless irrigated. You don't need a full pedology simulation — just enough to produce an agricultural potential map that varies coherently with the terrain.

What Agriculture Produces for City Generation
Hinterland carrying capacity — how many people can the surrounding region feed per unit area. High-productivity agricultural land (river valley alluvium, loess plains, well-watered lowlands) supports dense rural settlement and large cities. Low-productivity land (uplands, thin soils, arid margins) supports sparse settlement and small market towns with wide catchments.

Surplus type — what the land produces in excess of local consumption. This determines what the city trades and processes:

Grain surplus       → mill towns, grain markets, bread trades
Wool / livestock    → textile towns, tanneries, leather trades  
Wine / fruit        → processing and storage, specialist merchants
Timber              → sawmills, shipbuilding, construction trades
Fish (coastal)      → processing, salting, drying infrastructure
Specialist crops    → depends on crop (hops → brewing, flax → linen etc)
The surplus type drives which processing industries dominate the city's early economy, which in turn drives plot sizes, industrial district location, and the character of the warehouse district.

Market town spacing — there's a well-established relationship between agricultural hinterland productivity and market town spacing. In fertile lowlands, market towns are close together because each can draw on a rich but small catchment. In poor uplands, market towns are further apart because each needs a larger catchment to be viable. This is essentially central place theory — and it's derivable from your agricultural potential map rather than needing to be set independently.

Seasonality — when the agricultural surplus is produced drives the rhythm of the city. A grain harvest in late summer produces an autumn market peak. A wool clip in spring produces a spring fair. These seasonal pulses drive which buildings get built (seasonal storage, fair grounds, drovers' inns) and where — typically just outside the town boundary where seasonal overflow could be accommodated.

Weather's Specific Effects on Agriculture
The macroclimate parameters drive agricultural potential, but the relationship has some non-obvious features worth modelling:

Rainfall timing matters more than total rainfall. Mediterranean climates with dry summers and wet winters produce a completely different agricultural system from temperate climates with year-round rainfall — different crops, different storage requirements, different seasonal rhythms. A city in a Mediterranean climate needs large grain stores to bridge the summer dry season; a city in a temperate climate needs drainage infrastructure to manage waterlogged winter fields.

Temperature range drives what can be grown. Frost-free days determine whether you can grow wine grapes, citrus, or olives. The boundary of reliable winter wheat cultivation moves with temperature. These thresholds are sharp — a slight shift in the temperature parameter moves a region from wine-producing to grain-producing, which completely changes the city's trading character.

Extreme events create agricultural crises which drive urban episodes. A drought kills the harvest and drives famine migration into cities. A flood destroys lowland crops and drives food price spikes. A late frost kills the wine harvest for a year. These are shorter-cycle versions of the weather episodes — they don't reshape the city physically but they drive economic shocks that feed into the land value and economy layers, which then influence subsequent development episodes.

Irrigation changes the equation. Arid climates that would otherwise support minimal settlement can support dense urban populations if irrigation is available. The entire urbanism of Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, and the American Southwest is predicated on irrigation overriding the base climate constraint. If your terrain has rivers crossing arid land, irrigation potential is a significant modifier on agricultural productivity — and the hydraulic infrastructure required (canals, weirs, water rights administration) generates its own urban institutions and spatial patterns.

Agricultural Land as a City Use
Within the city boundary, agriculture appears in several forms at different scales:

Urban fringe market gardens — the belt of intensive vegetable and fruit production that historically surrounded most cities, supplying fresh produce that couldn't survive long transport. This land is always under development pressure and tends to be the first agricultural land absorbed into urban expansion. Its plots are typically medium-sized, irregular, and oriented toward access rather than frontage — the opposite of urban plot logic.

Infield strips and closes — remnant agricultural land inside the urban boundary, either not yet developed or preserved by institutional ownership. Often visible as anomalously large, irregular plots with curved boundaries betraying their origin as enclosed strips.

Orchards and nurseries — productive but semi-permanent, often on institutional land (monastery orchards, estate kitchen gardens). Tend to persist longer than market gardens because their productive cycle is longer and their institutional owners less responsive to development pressure.

Allotments — a specifically urban form of productive land, typically municipal, typically on marginal or temporarily unused land. A useful transitional use indicator in the generator — allotments appear where development pressure is low and disappear when it rises.

For the Generator
Agriculture probably needs to be an explicit layer in the regional pipeline, sitting between climate/terrain and settlement placement:

agriculturalPotential(terrain, climate, hydrology) -> AgriculturalMap {
  productivity:    Float per cell      -- drives hinterland carrying capacity
  surplusType:     SurplusType per cell -- drives processing industries
  seasonality:     SeasonalProfile     -- drives market rhythm
  irrigationPotential: Float per cell  -- modifier for arid areas with water access
}
From this map you can derive:

Where settlements are viable and how large they can grow before needing trade
What processing industries each settlement develops
How far apart market towns are spaced
What the seasonal rhythm of commercial activity is
Which land on the urban fringe is under agricultural use and therefore available for development episodes
The agricultural map also gives you the rural hinterland character that surrounds each city — which matters for the visual and narrative coherence of the region even if you're not generating the rural settlements in detail. A city surrounded by vineyards on terraced slopes looks and behaves differently from one surrounded by open grain fields or upland sheep pasture, and that difference should flow naturally from the terrain and climate rather than being set independently.

It's worth being precise about what these two things are:

Internal geography — properties of the region itself, derivable from your generation layers. Geology, terrain, hydrology, climate, soil, resources. These are spatially continuous across your map and vary cell by cell.

External pressure vectors — directional forces arriving from off-map. These aren't properties of the region itself but of its position in a larger world. They arrive from specific directions and with specific intensities, and they determine what infrastructure gets built to connect the region to that wider world.

The key insight is that external pressures are directional — they come from somewhere specific and go somewhere specific. That directionality is what determines where infrastructure enters and exits your map, which then determines which settlements sit on through-routes versus dead ends, which is one of the most powerful determinants of urban growth.

The Cardinal Pressure System
You could model external pressures as a set of vectors on each map edge, or more simply as a set of named pressures each with a direction and intensity:

ExternalPressures {
  
  -- Trade and movement pressures
  shippingLanes:     EdgePressure[]    -- which coast segments face busy sea lanes
  overlandRoutes:    EdgePressure[]    -- which map edges connect to major destinations
  riverConnections:  EdgePressure[]    -- which rivers connect to larger networks off-map
  
  -- Political pressures  
  capitalDirection:  DirectedPressure? -- where the political centre is
  imperialPresence:  DirectedPressure? -- colonial or imperial power direction
  borderProximity:   EdgePressure[]    -- which edges are near contested boundaries
  
  -- Economic pressures
  industrialDemand:  ResourceDemand[]  -- what the off-map world wants from this region
  foodDemand:        Float             -- how much agricultural surplus can be exported
  energyDemand:      Float             -- coal, oil, hydro demand from outside
  
  -- Threat pressures
  militaryThreat:    DirectedPressure? -- direction of potential invasion or conflict
  pirateRisk:        EdgePressure[]    -- which coasts face maritime threat
}
Each EdgePressure has a direction (which map edge or edge segment it acts on) and an intensity (how strong the pull or push is). Each DirectedPressure has a bearing (compass direction) and an intensity.

How Pressures Generate Infrastructure
The infrastructure layer is largely derivable from the external pressure field combined with internal geography. The logic is:

High-intensity pressure + viable route through terrain = infrastructure gets built

The type of infrastructure depends on the era and the pressure type:

shippingLane pressure    → harbour construction, then port facilities
overland route pressure  → road, then turnpike, then railway, then motorway
river connection         → wharf, then canal improvement, then navigation works
capital direction        → administrative road, then railway to capital
industrial demand        → mineral railway, then branch line, then standard gauge
military threat          → fortification, then strategic road, then garrison
The route the infrastructure takes through the region is determined by terrain — least cost path through the landscape, avoiding steep gradients for railways, seeking river crossings at narrows for roads. This is already the kind of thing your pipeline can probably derive.

Intersection Logic
The intersection question — whether a city sits at a busy crossing of routes — falls naturally out of this system rather than needing to be set independently.

A settlement at the confluence of two external pressures from different directions becomes an intersection city almost inevitably. If there's a shipping lane to the south generating a north-south coastal route, and an overland trade pressure from east to west, the settlement where those two infrastructure lines cross becomes the intersection point. Its importance is directly proportional to the intensity of both pressures.

This produces a natural hierarchy:

Terminus city        -- one route arrives and stops (dead end)
Through city         -- one route passes through
Junction city        -- two routes cross (the classic market town / railway junction)
Hub city             -- three or more routes converge (major regional centre)
Gateway city         -- sits at the only viable crossing of a terrain barrier
The gateway city is particularly interesting — it derives its importance not from multiple pressures but from being the single viable point where a major pressure crosses a terrain obstacle. A mountain pass town, a bridge town at a difficult river crossing, a port at the only sheltered harbour on a hostile coast. These can be very significant cities despite sitting on only one route, because that route has no alternative.

Infrastructure Tiers by Era
The same external pressures generate different infrastructure in different eras, which is important for the chronology:

Era             Dominant Infrastructure    Typical Route Logic
--------        ----------------------     -------------------
Pre-modern      Tracks and roads           Follows ridgelines, avoids wet ground
Medieval        Improved roads, bridges    Seeks river crossings, market towns
Early modern    Turnpikes, canals          More direct, engineered crossings
Industrial      Railways                   Seeks flat gradients, cuts through terrain
20th century    Motorways                  Highest standard, bypasses historic centres
Contemporary    High speed rail            Very flat gradients, tunnels and viaducts
Each era potentially adds a new infrastructure layer following slightly different route logic. A city that was on the medieval road may or may not be on the railway — if the railway followed a different valley, the medieval town declines and a new railway town grows at a different location. This is historically extremely common and produces the characteristic pattern of bypassed historic towns adjacent to railway-era successors.

Connecting This to Settlement Hierarchy
The external pressure field, combined with internal geography, produces a natural settlement hierarchy that you don't need to set independently:

Primary cities — where the strongest external pressures intersect with the best internal geography. A deep natural harbour facing a busy shipping lane, at the mouth of a navigable river draining productive agricultural land, with a defendable hill nearby. These locations accumulate multiple founding purposes and grow large.

Secondary cities — where a single strong external pressure meets good internal geography. A river crossing on a major overland route. A harbour on a moderate shipping lane. A mining town where a valuable resource deposit meets external industrial demand.

Market towns — where internal agricultural productivity generates local surplus but external pressures are weak. Their size and spacing is determined by hinterland productivity rather than external connections.

Route settlements — where infrastructure passes through marginal geography with limited internal resources. Coaching inns, railway junctions, canal maintenance depots. Exist purely to service the route rather than to exploit local resources.

Residual settlements — where neither internal geography nor external pressures are particularly strong. Small villages serving purely local agricultural functions, with limited growth potential unless an external pressure arrives.

From this single parameter set, the rest of the pipeline — settlement placement, infrastructure routing, city founding purposes, growth episodes, morphological character — should be largely derivable rather than independently set. The region tells its own story from the parameters outward.

The Core Realisation

The Full Parameter Stack
Working from the outside in, the determining factors layer like this:

World position — where the region sits in a larger world that the generator doesn't model. Latitude, continentality, prevailing wind. These set the macroclimate baseline that everything else modifies.

External pressure field — directional forces arriving from off-map. Shipping lanes, overland trade routes, capital direction, military threat, industrial demand, religious significance. Internal geography — the properties of the region itself. Geology, terrain, hydrology. These don't determine what happens but they constrain where and how external pressures manifest spatially. The Episode Architecture
Once you have a city location with a founding purpose and a geographic character, the chronology loop runs episodes against it. Each episode is a typed transformation of the city state, composed from spatial primitives, triggered by the external pressure field and internal conditions of the era.

The key structural points:

Episodes are the unit of morphological change. Each one reads the current city state, applies a transformation, and writes a new state. They're composeable because they agree on the city state interface. They're independently developable because each has a fixture input and a testable output.

Era bundles the available episodes. Era is not just a year but a set of capabilities and pressures — transport technology, planning authority, finance capacity, military threat level — that determines which episodes can fire and with what probability. The same location in different eras produces different episodes and therefore different morphology.

The chronology produces layering. A city is the accumulated result of all its episodes. The medieval organic core, the Georgian estate district, the railway cut, the modernist clearance, the contemporary infill — each is a legible episode layer that persists in the morphology even as later episodes modify it. Age determines how many layers exist and how complex the result is.

Organic growth is the background process. Between punctuating episodes, organic growth runs continuously — individual agents making plot-level decisions driven by land value gradients and era constraints. It fills in the gaps that planned episodes leave and produces the fine-grain variation that makes the fabric feel real rather than templated.

The Ownership and Legal Layer
Running through all of this is an ownership structure that the geography and external pressures don't determine directly but that strongly mediates how pressures translate into morphology.

Who owns land at the moment of development is the single most powerful predictor of plot grain and block size. Unified ownership produces coarse grain and planned layouts. Fragmented ownership produces fine grain and organic layouts. Institutional ownership produces permanent large plots that resist development pressure for centuries.

Tenure system determines whether land fragments over time (freehold sale) or stays unified (leasehold estate, religious endowment, state ownership). This is partly a cultural and legal parameter set by the founding conditions, and partly a function of which episodes have run — an estate development episode creates a temporarily unified ownership structure that eventually fragments as leases expire.

Roads are the boundary condition between public and private. New roads are created through subdivision when planned episodes run. In planned subdivision roads come first and plots are organised around them. In organic subdivision plots come first and roads appear only when access demands it. The morphological signature of each is distinct and readable in the resulting fabric.

Land Use as Episode Output
Land uses aren't assigned to plots directly — they emerge from the interaction of external pressures, era, and local conditions.

Different use types have different plot logics: frontage-dependent uses cluster at high-value street intersections and drive fine grain; footprint-dependent uses require amalgamation and appear on cleared or edge sites; precinct-forming uses reorganise entire districts around themselves. Uses mix vertically in pre-zoning and contemporary eras and separate into pure-use districts in mid-20th century planning — so the planning era is a parameter on how use is distributed across the plot stock.

Permanent uses — cemeteries, major parks, cathedrals — once placed are essentially fixed points that all subsequent episodes route around. Transitional uses — surface car parks, vacant lots, market gardens on the urban fringe — signal where development pressure hasn't arrived yet and are the most likely sites for the next development episode.

The LLM Decision Layer
The episode sequence can be driven by a combination of deterministic rules and LLM reasoning. The deterministic layer handles hard constraints — episode preconditions, era availability, spatial validity. The LLM layer handles the qualitative reasoning — given what this city looks like right now, what would actually happen next?

The LLM is most useful when given rendered bitmap layers of the city state alongside a text context describing the era and available episodes. Vision input lets it read morphological character and spatial opportunity directly rather than receiving a pre-digested text summary — it can notice things that weren't explicitly encoded, which is what makes it more than a lookup table.

The discipline is that the LLM decides what and why; the spatial functions decide how and where. The LLM never emits geometry directly. Its output is always a structured episode selection with parameters that get validated and then executed by the deterministic pipeline.

What This Means for Building the System
The practical implication of working backwards to this understanding is that the build order becomes clearer:

The spatial primitives and episode functions are the stable core — they should be built first, tested in isolation with fixture inputs, and kept clean of decision logic. The city state interface is the contract they all agree on.

The founding conditions and external pressure field are the generative seed — a relatively small parameter set from which the entire character of a region and its cities should be derivable. Getting this right is more important than getting any individual episode right, because everything downstream depends on it.

The LLM decision layer is an enhancement on top of a working deterministic system — not a dependency. The chronology loop should work without it, producing plausible if less nuanced results, and the LLM layer should be addable without changing the episode architecture.

Founding Date and Planning Intention
Founding date is one of the most powerful single parameters because it determines which epochs the city passes through from birth rather than inheriting mid-stream. A city founded in 1850 never has a medieval layer — it starts in the industrial epoch and its oldest fabric reflects that directly. A city founded in 1900 in the American west may have a railway before it has a proper street network.

The planning intention at founding is a separate axis from date but correlates with it. Cities founded by deliberate acts — colonial administrations, railway companies, industrial enterprises, government decree — tend to have stronger geometric intention in their layout than cities that grew organically from a market or river crossing. The American grid west of the Appalachians is the most systematic example: the township and range survey imposed a regular grid on the land before any settlement existed, so even the smallest town has a gridded street pattern that reflects the survey rather than the terrain or any organic growth logic. This is fundamentally different from an English market town where the street pattern reflects centuries of incremental path-widening and plot negotiation.

The founding intention parameter probably needs two components: geometric regularity (how strongly a planned geometry was imposed at founding) and institutional author (who founded it and why — railway company, colonial government, religious order, speculative developer, organic growth). These together determine the baseline morphological character that all subsequent episodes modify.

Major Buildings and Landmark Structures
These are a distinct category from ordinary plot development because they're individual objects with city-scale significance rather than fabric. They deserve explicit treatment in the city state.

The main types:

Civic and governmental — town hall, courthouse, customs house, guildhall, parliament, palace. These are typically placed on or adjacent to the primary civic space — the market square, the main plaza, the ceremonial axis. They're large single buildings on amalgamated plots, usually the product of a civic episode rather than market development. Their placement is often intentional and symbolic — facing the square, closing a vista, marking the centre of institutional power.

Religious — cathedral, major mosque, temple complex, monastery. As discussed, these predate surrounding fabric in old cities and generate their own precinct logic. In newer cities they're placed by institutional decision. Their plot is almost always larger than their footprint because they need forecourt, churchyard, or close around them. They're effectively permanent once placed.

Cultural — opera house, concert hall, major theatre, museum, gallery, library. These are typically products of a civic enrichment episode in the 19th or early 20th century, often associated with a period of municipal pride or bourgeois patronage. They tend to cluster in a cultural district, either near the civic centre or on the edge of the historic core where large plots were available.

Educational — university buildings, schools, colleges. Universities in old cities generate campus precincts that resist surrounding development pressure. Schools are more distributed — one per residential neighbourhood at roughly the scale that walking distance dictates.

Healthcare — hospital complexes. These start as single buildings and grow into large irregular campus structures as wings are added over successive episodes. Their location tends to shift over time — medieval hospitals in the city centre, Victorian hospitals on the then-edge, modern hospitals further out where large plots are available and ambulance access is easy.

Commercial landmarks — covered market hall, department store, major hotel, exchange building. These are large single-ownership buildings that represent a commercial episode — a moment when sufficient capital concentrated to build at a scale beyond the normal plot. The Victorian market hall is the canonical example: a large steel and glass structure occupying an amalgamated block, replacing the open-air market that preceded it.

Industrial monuments — mill, factory, gasometer, power station, waterworks, grain silo. These are large structures whose form is entirely determined by their function. They tend to be placed at the urban fringe during their active life and absorbed into the city as it grows around them. When they become obsolete they're either demolished or converted — the power station to gallery, the mill to apartments — which is itself a regeneration episode.

For the generator, major buildings probably need to be point objects with associated plot and precinct rules rather than just plot uses. Each has a footprint, a precinct radius within which it influences surrounding land values and uses, and a permanence rating that determines how likely clearance episodes are to remove it.

Parks and Open Space
Parks are under-specified in most city generators but they're morphologically significant because they're large, permanent, and they strongly influence surrounding land values and development character.

The main types have different origins and therefore different spatial logic:

Organic open space — market squares, village greens, fairgrounds — emerge from use rather than planning. They're the residue of a use that required open space and resisted building over time. Their shape is irregular, reflecting the organic plot boundaries around them. They're typically in the historic core.

Civic parks — the product of a deliberate episode, usually in the 19th century, when municipal authorities or private philanthropists created formal green space as public infrastructure. Olmsted's parks in American cities, Victoria Park in London, the Prater in Vienna. These are large, formally designed, and usually placed at what was then the urban edge — which means they're now surrounded by dense development that they've helped make valuable.

Estate garden squares — private open space within a planned residential development, accessible only to surrounding residents. The Bloomsbury squares are the canonical example. These are a product of the estate development episode — the landowner reserved a central plot as a garden to increase the value of surrounding residential plots. They're geometrically regular, medium-sized, and distributed through the estate fabric.

Linear parks and promenades — along waterfronts, former walls, or railway lines converted to greenways. These are typically the product of an infrastructure conversion episode — when the original use (fortification, industrial waterfront, freight railway) became obsolete and the corridor was repurposed as public space rather than developed.

Playing fields and recreation grounds — large flat areas for sport. Often on marginal land (flood plains, former industrial sites) because their low land value use can only survive where development pressure is weak. Frequently threatened by development episodes when land values rise.

Cemeteries — functionally open space but with a specific character. Almost never redeveloped once established. Tend to be placed at the urban edge at the time of their founding, absorbed into the city as it grows, and eventually become anomalously large green spaces in otherwise dense areas.

For the generator, parks need a permanence flag and a land value influence radius. A large civic park raises surrounding land values significantly — the most expensive addresses in many cities are adjacent to major parks. This influence should propagate through the economy layer and drive higher-quality development episodes in the surrounding area.

Transport Infrastructure at City Scale
Railways, motorways, and airports are qualitatively different from roads because they're non-permeable barriers as well as connectors. A railway line or motorway cuts through the urban fabric and can only be crossed at specific points, which reorganises the city around those crossing points and creates distinct characters on each side.

Railways introduce several spatial elements simultaneously: the station (a major civic building and activity centre), the goods yard (a large industrial plot demanding warehouse and processing uses nearby), the line itself (a barrier that divides the city), the railway lands (the wide corridor of operational land that resists other development), and the induced development around stations (speculative residential and commercial development responding to the accessibility improvement). The railway episode is one of the most morphologically powerful in the whole sequence because it does all of these things at once.

Station placement is particularly important. A terminus station is typically placed at the edge of the historic core — the railway company couldn't afford to demolish more than necessary and faced political resistance to going further in. A through station is placed wherever the line naturally crosses the city. The difference matters enormously: a terminus generates a dense activity cluster at one point; a through station generates activity at a crossing point that may be anywhere in the city.

Motorways are the 20th century equivalent — non-permeable barriers, specific interchange points, induced development around interchanges. But their morphological effect is different from railways in important ways. Motorway interchanges attract low-density car-dependent development (retail parks, distribution centres, business parks) rather than the dense mixed-use development that railway stations attract. And motorways that pass through existing cities typically require clearance episodes of significant scale — the destruction of existing fabric to make way for the elevated or cut road is one of the most damaging morphological episodes in 20th century urban history.

Airports operate at a different scale from both. A regional airport is essentially a new city-scale use that generates its own surrounding development logic — hotels, logistics, business parks, eventually residential. Its relationship to the existing city is primarily via the road and rail connections between them rather than via physical adjacency. The airport episode probably fires late in the chronology (20th century onward) and generates a satellite development cluster at the airport edge that has a different morphological character from the historic city.

The external pressure field and founding conditions determine the city's purpose and character. The founding date sets which epochs apply from birth. The founding intention sets the baseline geometric regularity of the street pattern and plot grain.

The chronology loop runs episodes against this foundation. Each episode is one of several types: organic growth (background process), development episodes (estate, speculative, industrial, civic), infrastructure episodes (road, railway, canal, motorway, airport), landmark episodes (major buildings placed as individual objects), open space episodes (parks, squares, cemeteries established as permanent features), clearance episodes (fabric removed by fire, flood, war, planning), and decline episodes (shrinkage, abandonment, obsolescence).

The morphology that results is the accumulated record of all these episodes acting on a specific geography under specific external pressures — legible, layered, and coherent because every element traces back to a cause that the parameter stack produced.

Good question. The core observation is that the spec contains two fundamentally different kinds of content, and they compress differently.

The Two Content Types
Structural content — the relationships, principles, and causal logic. How founding date determines epoch entry point. Why unified ownership produces coarse grain. How external pressures generate infrastructure. This content is genuinely load-bearing — if you lose it, an LLM expanding the spec will invent plausible-sounding but wrong relationships.

Enumerative content — lists of instances. Types of founding purpose. Types of landmark buildings. Types of open space. Types of episode. This content is almost entirely recoverable by a capable LLM given just the category name and a brief framing. If you write "founding purpose types" an LLM will produce port, market town, fortress, religious centre, industrial, administrative, and most of the others unprompted.

The compression opportunity is almost entirely in the enumerative content. The structural content needs to stay.

Compression Approaches
Bare term. Replace a list entirely with its category name. "founding purpose types" instead of the full enumeration. Maximum compression, works well when the category is unambiguous and the LLM's prior knowledge closely matches what you want. Risk: the LLM fills in the list from general knowledge, which may not match your specific framing or may miss edge cases you care about.

Term plus framing constraint. "founding purpose types — include decay/obsolescence vulnerability as a property of each." You drop the list but keep the one structural insight that wouldn't be recovered automatically. This is probably the best tradeoff for most lists in the spec.

Term plus anomalies only. Keep only the entries that are non-obvious or that carry structural significance. For landmark types you might write "landmark types (include industrial monuments — edge location, conversion episode when obsolete)" and drop cathedral, town hall, hospital because those are recoverable. The anomaly flag tells the expander that this entry has specific behaviour worth attending to.

Hierarchical compression. Replace a flat list with a typed hierarchy that implies the list. "episode types: constructive / destructive / background" implies a three-way classification that the expander can populate. The hierarchy carries the structural logic; the instances are recovered from it. Works well when the list has genuine internal structure.

Cross-reference compression. Where a list in one section is derivable from content in another, replace it with a pointer. "infrastructure episode types — one per transport mode in epoch bundle" means you don't enumerate railway, canal, motorway, airport separately — they're implied by whatever the epoch system defines. Reduces redundancy and keeps the two sections in sync.

What Genuinely Can't Be Compressed
The causal chains are the hardest to compress because they're the least recoverable from general knowledge. "unified ownership produces coarse grain" is a non-obvious claim that an LLM expanding from scratch might not reconstruct correctly. "external pressure intensity × terrain viability → infrastructure route" is a structural relationship that needs to be stated.

Similarly, the discipline constraints — "LLM decides what and why, spatial functions decide how and where", "roads in planned subdivision come before plots" — are the kinds of things that feel obvious once stated but would be inverted or ignored in an expansion without them.

The practical test for whether something can be compressed: would a capable LLM, given only the compressed form and the surrounding structural context, reconstruct the original intent faithfully? If yes, compress. If there's a meaningful probability it would get it wrong in a consequential way, keep it.
